**On May 13, 2026, EU energy ministers met to confront a systemic vulnerability: the bloc's dependence on Chinese hardware for power storage, a risk that mirrors the fossil fuel chokepoints currently destabilizing the Strait of Hormuz.**

## The Convergence of Two Dependencies

The current crisis in the Strait of Hormuz is a diagnostic event. It reveals that Europe's energy security remains tethered to external chokepoints while the bloc transitions into a new form of hardware dependency. As discussed in the latest [ministerial meetings](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/13/energy-ministers-confront-europes-dependence-on-chinas-power-storage-dominance), the dominance of Chinese battery storage infrastructure creates a strategic bottleneck.

The European Commission has countered this by deploying the revised Electricity Market Design (EMD) rules to decouple consumer prices from gas and accelerate the integration of renewables. Parallel to this, the [Connecting Europe Facility (CEF)](https://energy.ec.europa.eu/news/235-key-cross-border-energy-projects-boost-europes-interconnectivity-2026-04-09_en) has identified 235 cross-border projects to boost interconnectivity. Patria's view is that these are the necessary instruments for a sovereign energy union.

The deployment of these tools is uneven. While the legal framework exists, national interests slow implementation. The "Sovereignty Gap" persists not because of a lack of legislation, but because the political will to integrate energy grids at a federal scale remains fragmented.

## The Path to Federal Energy Governance

The transition from a market of coordinated national grids to a single European energy entity is now a matter of strategic autonomy. The current legislative tools—the EMD and the Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA)—operate within a framework of voluntary alignment. This mechanism is too slow for the pace of geopolitical competition.

To bridge this gap, the EU must move beyond the "crisis-response" model established by REPowerEU toward a permanent federal energy authority. Such an entity would not merely coordinate, but govern the strategic deployment of infrastructure. This ensures that energy sovereignty becomes a shared European asset rather than a fragmented national pursuit.
