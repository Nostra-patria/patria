**An independent European deterrent requires an annual budgetary increase of approximately €250 billion to move from basic maintenance to peer-level deterrence.**

## The Fiscal Reality of Independence

The ambition of "strategic autonomy" has long been a staple of European diplomatic discourse. The quantitative baseline for its realization is now clear. According to analysis from [Bruegel and the Kiel Institute](https://www.bruegel.org/analysis/defending-europe-without-us-first-estimates-what-needed), defending Europe without US support requires raising defense spending from roughly 2% to 3.5% of GDP. For Germany, the bloc's largest economy, this implies increasing national spending from €80 billion to €140 billion, alongside significant contributions to a shared EU-level funding pool.

This fiscal requirement exposes a structural friction. While the EU Defence Investment Framework targets 40% joint procurement by 2027, the reality remains fragmented. As of 2022, only 18% of defense investment was collaborative. The persistence of "national champions" drives this stagnation. Member states prioritize domestic industrial bases and local job creation over the economies of scale required for a unified strategic capability.

The result is a capability gap that is not merely financial, but institutional. Europe possesses the political desire for independence but lacks the federalized investment framework to execute it. Coordination has a structural ceiling.

## The Deterrent Asymmetry

The structural cornerstone of European autonomy currently rests on a single national capability. France's updated nuclear strategy, articulated by President Emmanuel Macron, emphasizes that Europe must be "feared" to be "free" [France 24](https://www.france24.com/en/france/20260302-macron-unveils-france-nuclear-strategy-eu-counter-russian-aggression-wavering-us). This provides the only hard deterrent available in the absence of US guarantees. However, it introduces a profound political asymmetry.

Because the nuclear deterrent remains under exclusive French national command, other EU member states face a binary choice: accept a "European" guarantee that is, in effect, a French national decision, or maintain strategic dependence on the United States.

This asymmetry is a structural flaw in the pursuit of autonomy. The current EU Defence Investment Framework attempts to bridge this gap through "variable geometry" and incremental collaboration. These tools cannot simulate a unified strategic command. The capability exists. The shared political authority to deploy it does not.

## The Industrial Friction

The transition from national spending to EU-scale procurement is blocked by the persistence of "national champions." Despite the EU's target of 40% joint procurement by 2027, the actual rate of collaborative investment stood at only 18% as of 2022 [EPRS](https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/13/eu-joint-defence-procurement/). Member states continue to prioritize domestic industrial bases over the interoperability and economies of scale required for a peer-level deterrent.

This protectionism creates a cycle of inefficiency. As noted by the European Parliament's Budgetary Support Unit, national budget increases alone risk reinforcing industrial fragmentation and amplifying duplication [European Parliament](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2026/783710/BUDG_BRI(2026)783710_EN.pdf). The structural logic suggests that without a federalized procurement authority, spending increases will simply subsidize twenty-seven competing industries rather than one unified force. Scale is the objective. Fragmentation is the reality.

## The Legitimacy Gap

The current push for a "European Army" is haunted by the failure of the European Defence Community (EDC) in 1954. The EDC failed not because of a lack of technical planning, but because it attempted a supranational integration of military forces without a corresponding democratic mandate [Verfassungsblog](https://verfassungsblog.de/why-the-european-defence-community-can-be-revived/). The French National Assembly's rejection confirmed that the monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force cannot be delegated to a nascent supranational body without deep political integration.

Modern efforts, such as PESCO and the European Defence Fund, attempt to avoid this deadlock through incrementalism. By collaborating on specific capabilities rather than a total surrender of command, the EU avoids the "all-or-nothing" risk of the 1950s. However, this "variable geometry" approach has a ceiling. While it allows for joint procurement, it does not resolve the fundamental question of who "calls the shots" [SIEPS](https://sieps.se/en/publications/2026/year-who-calls-the-shots-institutional-and-political-dynamics-in-european-defence/). The institutional memory of the EDC remains a barrier to the federation required for true strategic autonomy.

## The Command Ceiling

The gap between European defense spending and actual strategic autonomy is not a failure of funding, but a failure of command. While the EU can incrementally increase joint procurement through PESCO or the European Defence Fund, these tools cannot synthesize a unified political will to use force.

Transitioning from a collection of national armies to a sovereign European deterrent would require a formal shift toward federation. This move remains politically stalled. Until the decision to "call the shots" is transferred to a shared authority, autonomy remains a fiscal target without a political head. The architecture is ready. The authority is not.
