**The European Council's shift from "Strategic Autonomy" to "Strategic Interdependence" marks a transition from the pursuit of self-sufficiency to the management of dependencies as a tool of power.**

## The Interdependence Pivot

The structural logic of European foreign policy is changing. For a decade, the EU operated under the banner of "Strategic Autonomy," a framework defined in 2016 as the capacity to act independently when necessary. In practice, this manifested as a reactive effort to reduce vulnerabilities. Member states like Poland and the Netherlands viewed this as a drift toward protectionism or a French-German project for industrial hegemony. According to the [European Council on Foreign Relations (ECFR)](https://ecfr.eu/article/onwards-and-outwards-why-the-eu-needs-to-move-from-strategic-autonomy-to-strategic-interdependence/), this model reached its limit; it treated interdependence as a vulnerability to be erased rather than a reality to be governed.

The new paradigm, "Strategic Interdependence," acknowledges that total autonomy is a geopolitical fiction. The goal is now to proactively choose "when and how to depend on whom." This shift is not merely semantic. It is a structural admission that Europe's global influence depends on its ability to formalize ties with key allies while surgically reducing dependencies on rivals.

This recalibration is visible in the current approach to Ukraine. The proposal by CDU leader Friedrich Merz for Ukraine to become an EU associate member without voting rights [France 24](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20260521-germany-s-merz-proposes-ukraine-as-eu-associate-member-without-voting-rights) exemplifies this logic. By creating a formal institutional link that bypasses the paralysis of Council voting, the EU anchors Ukraine's technological and military agility into its own architecture without compromising institutional stability. The objective is no longer to isolate the Union from external shocks. It is to integrate the most critical partners into a shared structural defense.

## The NATO-EU Architectural Friction

The pursuit of strategic interdependence operates within a fragile structural overlap between the EU and NATO. While 23 member states share both memberships, the two organizations differ fundamentally: the EU is a political-economic union, while NATO is an operational-military alliance. The current tension centers on the creation of a "European pillar" within NATO—a structure intended to be credible to the United States while remaining autonomous enough to prevent total security dependence.

Under pressure from US policy shifts, particularly the "Trump 2.0" context, the automaticity of security guarantees is no longer a given. This has forced the EU to accelerate its own capabilities, yet the process remains fragmented. Member states often hedge their bets, splitting resources between EU initiatives and NATO command structures. This creates a risk of fragmented security, where the speed of crisis reaction is slowed by competing institutional loyalties. The capability gap is real. The institutional coordination is not.

## The Global South Credibility Gap

Europe's attempt to project influence beyond its borders is hindered by a divergence between its normative rhetoric and its realpolitik. The EU's "normative power"—the use of human rights and democratic values as a diplomatic lever—is increasingly perceived as conditional rather than a genuine partnership. This perception deepens when the EU pursues energy deals with autocratic regimes, creating a hypocrisy that erodes trust.

Structurally, the EU has struggled to match the material speed and scale of the China-led "infrastructure-first" development model. The transition from a "donor-recipient" model to a "partnership of equals" requires more than diplomatic language; it requires aligning [Global Gateway investments](https://www.realinstitutoelcano.org/en/analyses/europe-and-the-global-south-how-to-gain-influence-and-credibility-in-a-complex-world/) with the actual priorities of the Global South. Closing this gap is not a matter of better communication. It is a matter of structural reform in global governance, including the UN and WTO, to grant emerging powers a meaningful voice.

## The Logic of Controlled Dependency

The transition to strategic interdependence is a response to the failures of the "Strategic Autonomy" framework established in 2016. By moving away from the fiction of total autonomy, the EU acknowledges that power in a multipolar world is not the absence of dependency, but the ability to choose its terms. As the [European Council on Foreign Relations (ECFR)](https://ecfr.eu/article/onwards-and-outwards-why-the-eu-needs-to-move-from-strategic-autonomy-to-strategic-interdependence/) argues, this shift replaces the fiction of isolation with the logic of controlled dependency.

However, this transition is a prerequisite for influence, not a guarantee of it. It requires a level of institutional coherence that the Union currently lacks, as member states continue to hedge between Brussels and Washington. The result is a structural tension between a proactive global strategy and a fragmented security reality. The coordination gap remains.
