**European Commissioner for Defence Andrius Kubilius estimates that replacing US military assets withdrawn from the continent will cost €500 billion. This figure transforms strategic autonomy from a theoretical preference into a structural fiscal emergency.**

## The Cost of Security Dependence

The scale of the US asset replacement gap is no longer a matter of diplomatic speculation. Kubilius reports that the Trump administration's withdrawal of military capabilities has left a void requiring immediate expenditure and a shift toward joint production of strategic assets. The European Commission launched the [European Defence Industry Programme (EDIP)](https://defence-industry-space.ec.europa.eu/edip-commission-adopts-eu15-billion-work-programme-boost-european-and-ukrainian-defence-industry-2026-03-30_en) with a €1.5 billion work programme for 2026-2027. These grants act as catalysts. They cannot bridge a half-trillion-euro deficit.

To address this, the EU relies on the [Security Action for Europe (SAFE) fund](https://continents.tendersgo.com/post/eea-defence-initiatives-target-40-joint-procurement-by-2027-7613), which allocates €150 billion through 2030 via EU bonds to incentivize joint procurement. The target is 40% joint acquisition by the end of 2027. However, the obstacle is structural. A 2022 coordinated annual review revealed that only 18% of defence investment was collaborative, far below the 35% benchmark established in 2007. Ambition for scale is colliding with national fragmentation.

## The Article 346 Firewall

The structural deficit in European defence is not merely a lack of funding, but a legal one. Article 346 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) provides a "national security exception" that allows Member States to bypass standard EU procurement rules regarding transparency and non-discrimination. This creates a legal firewall. It enables states to prioritize national champions and domestic industries over single-market efficiency.

The result is a persistent fragmentation of the defence market. According to an [EPRS Briefing](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2026/785665/EPRS_BRI(2026)785665_EN.pdf), the 2022 review showed that collaborative investment remained at 18%. The legal architecture protects sovereignty, but the pattern suggests it now functions as a barrier to scale.

## Procurement Fault Lines

The push toward the 40% joint acquisition target by 2027 is colliding with national interests. While the SAFE fund provides €150 billion in loans, the appetite for supranationalism varies across the bloc. The tension lies between the need for "big items"—strategic capabilities that no single state can afford—and the desire to maintain domestic industrial bases.

Current initiatives like [BraveTech EU](https://defence-industry-space.ec.europa.eu/edip-commission-adopts-eu15-billion-work-programme-boost-european-and-ukrainian-defence-industry-2026-03-30_en) focus on urgent, small-scale innovations for Ukraine, but the structural challenge remains the "big spend." The fiscal space exists, supported by a Stability and Growth Pact "escape clause" allowing a 1.5% GDP increase in defence spending. Fiscal capacity does not automatically translate into political alignment. Coordination is possible; integration is contested.

## The Shadow of the 1952 EDC

The struggle to bridge the €500 billion gap echoes the failure of the 1952 European Defence Community (EDC). The EDC attempted to create a supranational army, but it collapsed because Member States refused to cede the core of their national sovereignty. As noted in [Verfassungsblog analysis](https://verfassungsblog.de/again-the-european-defence-community-is-dead/), Article 346 TFEU effectively encodes this same refusal to transition from a collection of national armies to a unified force.

To avoid this historical dead-end, new frameworks are emerging. The [CEPS "Roadmap Towards a Common Defence"](https://cdn.ceps.eu/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/ROADMAP-TOWARDS-A-COMMON-DEFENCE-FOR-EUROPE_V4.3.pdf) proposes a spectrum of governance models that move incrementally toward supranational integration. This shift is being monitored by external powers. Beijing, for instance, views the progression of Strategic Autonomy as the primary indicator of whether Europe is becoming a cohesive security provider or remaining a dependent consumer. The goal is no longer just a shared budget, but a shared command.

## The Sovereignty Ceiling

The €500 billion gap is a fiscal reality, but the barrier to closing it is institutional. As long as Article 346 TFEU allows Member States to treat defence procurement as a national sanctuary, the EU will operate as a collection of twenty-seven fragmented markets rather than a single strategic actor. Moving toward the CEPS roadmap's supranational models would require a political trade-off that currently lacks a majority. The result is a persistent tension between the need for European-scale capabilities and the desire for national control. Coordination has a structural ceiling.
