**The European Union fails to return approximately 75% of third-country nationals issued with a deportation order. The new Common European System for Returns attempts to close this gap by treating a return decision in one member state as a mandatory mandate for all others.**

## From National Discretion to Mutual Recognition

The political agreement reached on June 2, 2026, regarding the new Return Regulation, introduces the European Return Order (ERO) as a central enforcement tool. Previous frameworks under Directive 2008/115/EC relied on fragmented national transpositions and optional cooperation. The ERO establishes a regime of mandatory mutual recognition. Under this system, a return decision issued by one member state is automatically recognized and enforceable in another. This removes the administrative redundancy where national bureaucracies previously issued new decisions for the same individual as they moved across borders [European Parliament](https://epthinktank.eu/2026/06/16/a-common-system-for-the-return-of-third-country-nationals-staying-illegally-in-the-european-union-eu-legislation-in-progress-2/).

This shift serves as the operational back-end to the broader Migration and Asylum Pact. On June 12, 2026, new rules on screening and external border management entered application, requiring immediate identity and security checks for all irregular entrants [European Commission](https://home-affairs.ec.europa.eu/news/new-migration-and-asylum-rules-enter-application-what-changing-2026-06-12_en). By identifying return-eligible individuals at the first point of entry, the EU aims to feed the ERO mechanism instantly. It bypasses the years of judicial and administrative drift that typically characterize national return processes.

For EU Commissioner Magnus Brunner, this federalization of enforcement is the price of internal seamlessness. Brunner frames the firm external border and the efficiency of the return system as the necessary conditions to justify the removal of internal Schengen border checks, which several member states—including France, Germany, and Italy—have maintained despite EU pressure [The Local](https://www.thelocal.com/20260602/eu-calls-on-nine-european-countries-to-scrap-internal-schengen-border-checks). The logic is structural: the EU cannot restore the Schengen ideal if the member states cannot agree on who is legally permitted to stay.

## The Mutual Recognition Mechanism

The ERO targets the systemic fragmentation that has historically undermined EU migration policy. The European Policy Centre notes that divergent transposition of previous directives and conflicting national court decisions created a landscape where similar cases led to different outcomes across the Union [European Policy Centre](https://www.epc.eu/publication/returns-under-the-spotlight-towards-an-effective-common-eu-system/). By standardizing the order, the EU attempts to remove the implementation gap used by national administrations to avoid the political and logistical cost of returns.

This mechanism creates a seamless enforcement chain. When a screening officer identifies an individual as return-eligible under the June 12 rules, the ERO ensures that the resulting deportation order follows the person across every internal border. Administrative friction is removed.

## National Friction and the Polish Case

The move toward federalized returns creates a clear divide between those seeking operational efficiency and those resisting the loss of national discretion. Poland represents the primary point of friction. The Polish administration seeks to lead the Union in maximizing the volume of returns, yet it simultaneously resists the broader obligations of the New Pact for Migration and Asylum. This implies a desire for the tools of federal enforcement without the constraints of federal solidarity.

Other member states have historically used divergent national practices to shield their domestic judicial systems from EU mandates. As noted by the European Parliament, only about a quarter of third-country nationals with a return decision actually leave the EU. This failure is rarely a lack of intent; it is a result of national courts widening the gaps in how EU law is applied.

The ERO attempts to close these gaps by making the European nature of the order superior to national administrative preference. However, the effectiveness of this approach depends on whether member states accept the loss of their veto over the return process. If national courts continue to challenge the validity of orders issued by other states, the ERO will become another layer of legal conflict rather than a solution to it.

## The Trade-off of Accelerated Procedures

The push for efficiency introduces a conflict between operational speed and legal protection. To make the ERO viable, the new regulation relies on accelerated procedures to identify and process return-eligible individuals quickly. The OHCHR has warned that this acceleration may erode the right to an effective legal remedy and increase the risk of refoulement—returning individuals to territories where they face persecution or torture.

This is the central trade-off of the federalized approach: the gain in administrative velocity comes at the cost of individual legal security. The structural driver of return failure is not merely inertia, but a genuine dependency on third-country cooperation. No matter how efficient the ERO is internally, a return cannot occur if the country of origin refuses to accept the individual. The ERO solves the internal administrative problem, but it cannot solve the external diplomatic one.

The risk is that the EU creates a high-speed deportation machine that remains stalled at the external border. If the firm return mandate leads to systemic violations of non-refoulement, the resulting legal challenges in the European Court of Human Rights could paralyze the system entirely. The efficiency of the mechanism does not eliminate the volatility of the law.

## The Enforcement Ceiling

The European Return Order solves an internal administrative failure, but it cannot override the external reality of diplomatic dependence. A federalized return mechanism ensures that a deportation order travels seamlessly across the Schengen area, yet it remains powerless if the country of origin refuses readmission. This creates a structural ceiling: the EU has built a high-speed internal engine for returns that still relies on a fragmented network of bilateral readmission agreements. The effectiveness of the ERO now depends on whether the Commission can leverage the Union's collective economic weight to force third-country cooperation. Without these agreements, the ERO is merely a more efficient way to document a failure. The gap between legislative ambition and physical reality remains.
