**The European External Action Service (EEAS) operates as an institutional hybrid that prioritizes consensus over agility, leaving member states to reclaim their bilateral agency in a multipolar world.**

## The Shift to Economic Security

The EU's global role is transitioning from a "normative power" that exports values to a transactional actor focused on economic security. This paradigm shift, detailed in recent [EPRS analysis](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2026/783766/EPRS_BRI(2026)783766_EN.pdf), treats trade, investment, and industry as strategic vulnerabilities rather than tools of cooperation. The *EU International Digital Strategy 2025* codifies this logic, pivoting from values-based digital diplomacy toward a framework of geopolitical competition.

The institutional architecture designed to project this power—the EEAS—cannot keep pace. Operational lag persists; while national capitals react to crises in hours, the EEAS often requires months to produce consensus-based documents. This inertia creates a structural ceiling. The need for great-power agility is blocked by a 27-nation veto model.

The result is a diplomatic unraveling. [ECFR data](https://foreignpolicy.pk/the-diplomatic-unraveling-why-europes-capitals-want-to-tear-apart-the-eeas/) shows that over 65% of senior diplomats in member capitals now consider bilateral channels more effective than Brussels-led coordination. From the "mini-lateralism" of the E3 to Germany's unsuccessful June 3, 2026, bid for a UN Security Council seat, the pattern is consistent: European capitals are bypassing the center to regain the speed required for global competition.

## The Institutional Hybrid

The EEAS is a structural anomaly, caught between the administrative funding of the European Commission and the political veto power of 27 member states. This hybridity creates systemic lag. National capitals react to geopolitical shifts in hours, yet the EEAS requires months of coordination for a single document. The service frequently functions as a redundant layer, diluting national priorities without adding distinct strategic value.

Internal reviews from October 2025 suggest that a significant portion of communications from EU delegations abroad merely duplicate reports from national embassies. This redundancy is a feature of an architecture that prioritizes the slow-moving compromise over the agility required for great-power competition. The institutional inertia has turned a geopolitical shield into a bureaucratic anchor.

## The Sovereign Reclamation

The current unraveling is driven by a clash between the interests of major powers and the needs of smaller states. For Paris and Berlin, the EEAS's inability to forge immediate consensus during security crises is no longer acceptable. This has led to "mini-lateralism," where small coalitions—such as the E3 (France, Germany, and the UK)—execute policy shifts rapidly by bypassing the 27-nation model.

A faction of foreign ministers is now seeking to reclaim sovereign power. According to [Foreign Policy](https://foreignpolicy.pk/the-diplomatic-unraveling-why-europes-capitals-want-to-tear-apart-the-eeas/), some efforts aim to strip the High Representative of operational command over joint missions and return that authority to intergovernmental committees. This push creates a dual-track foreign policy. National diplomats and EU envoys occasionally issue contradictory statements within the same 48-hour window. The divide is structural. Smaller states, such as Malta and Slovenia, remain dependent on the 145 EU delegations for global reach, while larger powers view the same apparatus as a constraint.

## The Shift from Normative to Transactional

The crisis of the EEAS is the physical manifestation of a deeper paradigm shift. For decades, the EU operated as a "normative power," exporting values and regulations through the "Brussels effect." However, the [EPRS analysis](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2026/783766/EPRS_BRI(2026)783766_EN.pdf) confirms a transition toward an "economic security" model. In this framework, trade, investment, and industry are strategic vulnerabilities or assets.

The *EU International Digital Strategy 2025* explicitly codifies this, moving away from values-based digital diplomacy toward a geopolitical and competition-driven approach. The priority has shifted from spreading digital regulations to securing physical infrastructure, such as submarine cables. This transactional logic prioritizes the mitigation of dependencies over global cooperation. The EU is no longer trying to convince the world to follow its rules; it is trying to ensure it is not vulnerable to the rules of others. The normative era has ended.

## The Structural Ceiling

The current crisis suggests that the EEAS was designed for normative influence, not great-power competition. Merging the service into the Commission to create a unified security council could bridge the gap between economic tools and diplomatic action. Such a move would require a fundamental treaty change and a willingness by member states to surrender the bilateral agency they are currently reclaiming. Until that political trade-off is accepted, the EU will continue to operate on two speeds: a slow, consensus-driven center and a fragmented, agile periphery. The architecture is the obstacle.
