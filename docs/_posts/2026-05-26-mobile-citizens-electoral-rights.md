**The June 2025 Council decision to strengthen the rules on the right to vote for mobile EU citizens acknowledges a structural failure: for millions, the legal right to vote is an administrative fiction.**

## The 2025 Shift in Electoral Rules

The Council's decision to amend the rules on the right to vote and stand as a candidate for mobile EU citizens attempts to close the "Democratic Gap." This gap is the distance between the legal entitlements guaranteed by [Article 22 of the TFEU](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A12002E) and the practical ability of a citizen to exercise those rights when living in a member state other than their own. Following a 2021 European Commission proposal and reports from the [Committee on Constitutional Affairs (AFCO)](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/committees/en/afco), the Council is moving toward a standardized implementation of Directive 93/109/EC.

The objective is to eliminate a fragmented landscape of democratic participation. Currently, mobile EU citizens—roughly 4% of the voting population—face a patchwork of registration deadlines, prohibitive documentation requirements, and a systemic lack of translated information. These are not technical glitches; they are institutional barriers that penalize mobility. The structural logic points to a simple conclusion: if the right to vote is treated as a diplomatic courtesy rather than a requirement for a functioning European demos, political identity cannot scale beyond the national border.

## The Administrative Friction

The "Democratic Gap" is not a product of missing legislation, but of flawed implementation. While [Article 39 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:12002) and [Article 22 of the TFEU](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A12002E) establish the legal entitlement, systemic administrative friction hampers the practical exercise of these rights. This manifests as a combination of information barriers and institutional ignorance. Many mobile citizens remain unaware of their rights because critical information regarding registration processes and political programs is rarely translated into multiple languages, as noted by [ECAS](https://ecas.org/stronger-rights-for-eu-mobile-citizens-in-european-parliament-elections/).

The failure is often local. Civil servants at the municipal level frequently lack the training to recognize the specific rights of mobile EU citizens. They fail to inform residents of their ability to vote or stand as candidates during the standard residency registration process. This institutional blindness transforms a legal right into a hidden privilege. The burden of discovery falls entirely on the individual.

The result is a participation gap. Mobile EU citizens consistently show lower turnout than nationals. The law exists; the access does not.

## The Fragmentation of Rights

The current application of [Directive 93/109/EC](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A3193L0109) has created a disparity where democratic rights vary by member state. The divergence is most evident in the registration process. Many states rely on "active registration," forcing citizens to navigate complex deadlines and prohibitive documentation requirements to prove residency or eligibility. This system penalizes those who move frequently for work or study, effectively disenfranchising the most mobile segment of the European population.

Resistance to standardizing these rules is frequently framed as a defense of national sovereignty. Certain conservative MEPs argue that these reforms push toward a centralized superstate by transferring competence from national capitals to Brussels. From this perspective, electoral rolls are not administrative lists but reflections of a national community.

The tension is between two different concepts of citizenship. One treats it as a national responsibility tied to tradition and language; the other treats it as a portable, structural feature of a European demos. The current fragmentation suggests that the Union is still operating as a coordination of states rather than a community of citizens.

## The Shift Toward Political Identity

The transition from the 2021 Commission proposal to the June 2025 Council decision marks a shift in the EU's structural logic. For decades, the right to vote for mobile citizens was treated as a diplomatic courtesy—a secondary right that national bureaucracy could undermine. The new objective is to shift the burden of registration from the individual to the state, moving toward models like automatic registration upon residency update.

This shift is not merely about increasing turnout; it is about the emergence of a transnational political identity. When the exercise of electoral rights is characterized by high fragmentation, as documented in the [MobilEU Project Report](https://orbi.uliege.be/bitstream/2268/328946/1/mobileu_policy_report_final-2023-2.pdf), the citizen remains a national actor operating in a foreign space. By removing administrative hurdles, the EU is attempting to create a structural environment where a citizen can be politically active regardless of their physical coordinates.

The goal is to move beyond the "union of states" model. If the right to vote becomes a seamless, portable feature of citizenship, the psychological barrier between "home" and "host" country begins to dissolve. The infrastructure for a European demos is being built.

## The Limit of Administrative Citizenship

The transition to a portable electoral right is a necessary condition for a European demos, but it is not a sufficient one. Standardizing registration removes the friction of the "Democratic Gap," yet it does not automatically create the political demand for a transnational identity. The shift from a union of states to a union of citizens requires more than the removal of bureaucratic hurdles; it requires a political class capable of speaking to a mobile electorate that no longer fits into a single national box. 

The infrastructure is arriving. The political identity remains in draft.
