**The European Union manages a legislative agenda for a superpower but operates on a budget representing roughly 1% of its GDP. This structural mismatch means that while the Union mandates continental standards, it lacks the autonomous revenue to enforce them without relying on the treasuries of its member states.**

## The Budgetary Gap and the CORE Proposal

The tension between strategic ambition and financial capacity has reached a critical point in 2026. To repay NextGenerationEU debt and finance the green and digital transitions, the European Commission proposed the Corporate Resource for Europe (CORE) for the 2028-2034 Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF). CORE targets companies with a net annual turnover of at least €100 million, capping contributions at €750,000 to generate approximately €6.8 billion annually [LSE Europp Blog](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/europpblog/2025/08/28/the-commissions-foray-into-corporate-taxation-a-core-problem-for-the-eu-budget/).

This proposal attempts to move the Union toward "own resources" and reduce dependence on GNI-based national contributions. The erosion of the EU's financial autonomy is quantifiable: the VAT own resource provided 60% of the budget in 1988, but fell to 9% by 2023 [Tax Foundation](https://taxfoundation.org/blog/eu-budget-tax-core/). By tapping directly into the economic value generated by large corporations, CORE would establish the Union as a sovereign fiscal actor.

Member states, most notably Germany, resist the proposal, viewing any direct EU tax as an encroachment on national tax sovereignty. This deadlock persists because the EU's budget is not merely a financial ledger, but a reflection of its political architecture. Coordination is possible. Governance requires revenue.

## The Pillar 2 Leeway and Industrial Policy

The 2026 OECD amendments to the global minimum tax introduced a specific technical flexibility: the Substance-based Tax Incentive Safe Harbour (SBTI-SH). Under Pillar 2, this mechanism allows member states to grant tax breaks linked to real economic activity—specifically payroll and tangible assets—without triggering "top-up taxes" that would otherwise enforce the 15% global floor [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/analysis/how-global-minimum-tax-amendments-could-reshape-europes-tax-incentives). By protecting incentives tied to substance, the SBTI-SH allows the EU to accommodate decarbonization and strategic investment goals within a restrictive global framework.

This leeway is not distributed equally. Germany and France possess the greatest absolute capacity to utilize these incentives, while smaller economies like Luxembourg rely more heavily on asset-based options [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/analysis/how-global-minimum-tax-amendments-could-reshape-europes-tax-incentives). The pattern suggests a new form of fragmented competition. While the global floor exists, the "substance" loophole allows the largest EU economies to continue competing for industrial projects through targeted tax relief. The minimum tax is a floor, but the ceiling for competition remains open.

## The Flexibility of the Reformed Fiscal Framework

Parallel to corporate tax tensions, the EU has shifted its broader fiscal governance toward a tailored approach to preserve stability without stifling investment. The reformed fiscal framework now utilizes national medium-term fiscal structural plans (MTPs) featuring two primary flexibility mechanisms [Institut Delors](https://institutdelors.eu/content/uploads/2026/01/PP319_The_reformed_EU_fiscal_framework_in_action_Eisl_EN.pdf). The first is a "permanent" extension clause, allowing member states to delay deficit reduction if they commit to reforms aligned with EU strategic goals, such as the green or digital transitions. The second is a "temporary" national escape clause, designed to allow deviations from fiscal targets to fund defense spending.

This architecture departs from the rigidity of the previous Stability and Growth Pact. By aligning national spending with Union priorities, the EU is effectively attempting to steer national budgets from the center. Yet, these clauses risk becoming permanent escape hatches if the definition of "qualifying investments" remains too loose. The framework provides the tools for coordination. It does not yet provide the discipline of integration.

## The Relocation of Tax Authority

The defense of "tax sovereignty"—the authority to independently design and enforce tax laws—remains a core tenet of statehood for many member states [IBFD](https://www.ibfd.org/sites/default/files/2025-01/recent-eu-direct-tax-initiatives-the-end-of-tax-sovereignty.pdf). Legally, this is protected by the principle of conferral and the requirement for unanimity under Article 115 TFEU for any direct taxation harmonization. This legal barrier explains the fierce resistance from capitals like Berlin toward the CORE proposal.

Despite this, political authority is shifting through two alternative vectors. First, the EU uses its competence over the Internal Market (Article 4(2)(a) TFEU) to implement rulebooks like BEFIT (Business in Europe: Framework for Income Taxation), which standardizes the corporate tax base and strips states of their design autonomy [IBFD](https://www.ibfd.org/sites/default/files/2025-01/recent-eu-direct-tax-initiatives-the-end-of-tax-sovereignty.pdf). Second, authority is shifting toward external technocracies like the OECD, where "safe harbour" rules are defined via administrative guidance, bypassing national parliaments. Sovereignty is not being seized in a single legislative act; it is being eroded by administrative necessity.

## The Revenue Ceiling

The transition from a contribution-based budget to a sovereign-revenue model is not a matter of accounting, but of political will. While tools like CORE and BEFIT demonstrate a technical capacity for fiscal coordination, they operate within a legal framework designed to protect national treasuries. The current trajectory points to a hybrid state: the EU will continue to standardize the rules of the game through administrative guidance and internal market law, while the actual funding remains a fragmented negotiation between twenty-seven capitals. The structural gap persists. Governance without autonomous revenue is merely coordination.
