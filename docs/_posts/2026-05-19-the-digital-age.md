**The launch of the Frontier AI Initiative by France, Germany, and the European Commission on November 18, 2025, exposed a structural contradiction: the drive to build massive computational capacity is being used to justify the dismantling of the data protections that distinguish the EU from the US and Chinese models.**

## The Frontier AI Initiative and the Compute Gap

European AI holds a structural vulnerability, codified when France, Germany, and the European Commission launched the [Frontier AI Initiative](https://www.elysee.fr/en/emmanuel-macron/2025/11/18/germany-france-and-the-european-commission-launch-frontier-ai-initiative-at-the-summit-on-european-digital-sovereignty). The objective is a correction of the "compute gap"—the disparity in hardware, energy, and capital that threatens to reduce Europe to a vassal state of non-European providers. Mistral CEO Arthur Mensch argues that the window to avoid total dependency on US infrastructure is limited to two years before the supply chain of chips and electrons is fully monopolized.

This pursuit of capacity coincides with a legislative push to treat personal data as a negotiable asset. The European Commission is advancing a "Digital Omnibus" package and proposals for EU-US data exchange—which Statewatch reports may conflict with EU laws—to simplify AI training laws by redefining "personal data" and bypassing GDPR safeguards. The logic is simple: to compete in the AI race, the EU believes it must lower the cost of data acquisition.

The result is a paradox. The EU seeks strategic autonomy in hardware while voluntarily surrendering autonomy over digital rights. Sovereignty is being redefined as the possession of GPUs, rather than the protection of citizens.

## The Digital Omnibus and the Erosion of Rights

The pursuit of computational sovereignty now justifies a systemic rollback of digital protections. Through the "Digital Omnibus" package, the European Commission seeks to simplify the regulatory environment to boost competitiveness. The structural cost is a direct assault on the GDPR. The proposal redefines "personal data" to facilitate easier harvesting for AI training and introduces a "disproportionate efforts" carveout for data removal. This is a strategic pivot that treats the fundamental right to privacy as a friction to be removed.

The erosion extends to the AI Act. Proposals seek to remove the requirement for providers to publish risk assessments for high-risk systems. This shifts the EU from a precautionary model toward a permissive one. By treating personal data as a "negotiable asset" in EU-US data exchange proposals, the Commission trades the legal autonomy of European citizens for temporary access to American compute. The legal framework is being hollowed out from within.

## The Surveillance Split in the Council

The drive toward an "AI state" has exposed a divide within the Council of the EU, centered on the CSA Regulation and the scanning of private encrypted messages. A pro-surveillance bloc, led by Spain and Cyprus, pushes for broad access to encrypted communications. This reflects a security-first logic where the state's need for visibility outweighs the individual's right to privacy.

Conversely, a privacy-protection bloc, led by Germany and Austria, views the integrity of encryption as non-negotiable. Austria proposes scrapping scanning entirely, while Germany seeks to exclude encrypted messages from the regulation's scope. This tension is structural. The Council's own legal services warn that the current trajectory could compromise the essence of the rights to privacy and enable general, indiscriminate access to communications. The consensus on a "European way" of digital rights is fracturing.

## The Geopolitical Squeeze and the Vassal State

Europe's digital strategy is a reaction to a geopolitical squeeze. A US administration weaponizes tech dependency; China controls the critical materials for the hardware layer. This creates a vulnerability where Europe risks becoming a vassal state, importing digital intelligence without possessing the means of production. As Arthur Mensch noted, the ability to "transform electrons into tokens" is the only real lever of power in the AI era. Without massive investment in energy and chips, Europe has no leverage.

This desperation for capacity drives the "AI state" model, where efficiency justifies internal mass surveillance. Amnesty USA reports that AI-powered welfare systems in Denmark result in discrimination against marginalized groups. The pattern suggests that when a state lacks external leverage, it compensates by increasing internal control. The result is a digital model that mirrors the systems it claims to provide an alternative to. Sovereignty is being pursued at the cost of the citizen.

## The Sovereignty Trade-off

The drive for computational capacity is not a neutral technical upgrade, but a decision about the nature of the European state. By treating digital rights as a negotiable asset to secure hardware, the EU risks building a sovereign infrastructure that lacks a sovereign legal foundation. The tension between the Frontier AI Initiative and the Digital Omnibus indicates a strategy to trade the individual for the aggregate. 

The structural consequence is a model of autonomy that prioritizes the power of the state over the rights of the citizen. The distinction between the European and American models is disappearing. Acting as a collection of national interests, Europe can acquire hardware. Acting as a federation of rights, it could govern the technology. The choice between those two is now a concrete policy question.
