**The "big six" EU economies—Germany, France, Spain, Italy, Poland, and the Netherlands—have demanded a "completion package" for the Capital Markets Union to bypass institutional stagnation in Brussels.**

## From Diagnosis to Execution

The joint letter from the E6 marks a departure from the iterative, incremental regulation that defined the Capital Markets Union (CMU) for a decade. These economies are pushing for a structural transition from a "Savings Union," where capital remains trapped in national bank deposits, to an "Investment Union" capable of funding industrial scale at the European level. This move is anchored by Enrico Letta's proposed "One Market Act" [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/05/29/eus-six-largest-economies-push-for-capital-markets-union).

The urgency is driven by a hard temporal constraint: a proposed final deadline of 2028, with intermediate milestones in 2026 and 2027. This timeline transforms the CMU from a perpetual policy objective into a time-bound political mandate. The goal is to create a "28th regime"—a harmonized legal framework for contracts and insolvency that removes the uncertainty discouraging cross-border investment [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/first-glance/28th-regimes-help-europes-capital-markets).

Financial scaling is the prerequisite for the "Buy European" industrial push. To counter the US "Buy American" policy, the EU requires massive capital flows into strategic sectors—defence, semiconductors, and clean tech—that national markets cannot sustain individually. Without a unified capital market, the ambition of strategic autonomy remains a policy goal without a funding mechanism.

## Supervisory Fragmentation and the ESMA Gap

The structural failure of the Capital Markets Union to date lies in the distinction between regulatory harmonization and supervisory integration. While the EU has created uniform rules, the enforcement of those rules remains fragmented across twenty-seven National Competent Authorities (NCAs). The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) currently lacks the direct power to supervise market participants; it serves as a coordinator of national regulators. This architecture creates a persistent environment for regulatory arbitrage, where capital flows are governed by the weakest link in the supervisory chain rather than a single European standard.

The structural logic suggests that without a "Single Supervisor" model, the transition to an Investment Union is impossible. Direct supervisory powers for ESMA would remove the friction created by national silos, transforming the EU from a collection of fragmented ponds into a single ocean of liquidity. The architecture for coordination exists. The power to enforce does not.

## The Tipping Point of National Sovereignty

The primary political roadblock to this integration has been the defense of national supervisory sovereignty, led most prominently by Germany. The German model of national oversight has acted as a veto on the empowerment of ESMA. However, the German finance minister has signaled an openness to compromise on financial supervision [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/germany-open-compromise-eu-capital-markets-union-finance-minister-says-2026-05-26/). This represents a critical tipping point. By signaling a willingness to move beyond the NCA model, Germany removes the heaviest political weight opposing the "completion package" demanded by the E6.

This shift is a matter of strategic necessity. The "big six" economies recognize that the legislative path in Brussels has become a site of institutional stagnation [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/05/29/eus-six-largest-economies-push-for-capital-markets-union). The move toward a "One Market Act" is an attempt to bypass this inertia. The political will is aligning with the structural requirement: the need for scale outweighs the desire for national control.

## The Legacy of the Savings Union

Europe's current financial architecture is a legacy of the "Savings Union," where capital is trapped in low-risk national bank deposits rather than flowing into productive risk-capital. Previous attempts to solve this through iterative regulation failed because they ignored the legal fragmentation of the continent. The lack of a "28th regime"—a harmonized alternative to twenty-seven different national procedures for contracts and insolvency—means that investors face prohibitive uncertainty when a cross-border deal fails [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/first-glance/28th-regimes-help-europes-capital-markets).

This legal thicket ensures that European companies struggle to find scale-up funding, regardless of the quality of the underlying technology. The failure is not one of diagnosis, but of speed and ownership [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/05/29/eus-six-largest-economies-push-for-capital-markets-union). For a decade, the EU has treated the CMU as a perpetual strategy rather than a concrete project. The 2028 deadline is an admission that the incremental approach has failed.

## The Cost of Fragmentation

The transition to an Investment Union is a structural requirement for any credible "Buy European" strategy. If the EU cannot resolve the supervisory gap by 2028, the ambition of strategic autonomy will remain a series of unconnected national procurement lists. The political willingness of the E6 to bypass Brussels suggests a realization that the current legislative pace is incompatible with the speed of global industrial competition. The shift from national ponds to a single ocean of liquidity requires a transfer of sovereignty that no treaty has yet fully authorized. The question is no longer whether to integrate, but whether the cost of remaining fragmented has finally become unbearable.
