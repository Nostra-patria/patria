**The European Union operates as a federal power through the judicial expansion of its competences, yet it remains formally anchored in a confederation of sovereign states.**

## The Mechanics of Functional Federation

The structural tension of the Union is defined by the "vertical division of competences." Under [Article 5 of the Treaty on European Union (TEU)](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:12012E), the Union may act only within the limits of the powers conferred upon it by Member States. In practice, however, the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has consistently bypassed these formal constraints. By redefining the scope of existing competences rather than claiming new ones, the Court has shifted the boundary of EU power without the need for treaty amendments.

This process constitutes a "Silent Constitutional Revolution." Between the mid-1970s and mid-1980s, the principle of enumerated powers effectively collapsed, allowing the Union's material jurisdiction to expand cumulatively into nearly every area of governance. As noted in [European Parliament Working Paper W 26a](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/workingpapers/poli/w26/silent_en.htm), this erosion reached a point where no core of sovereign state power remains entirely beyond the reach of the center.

The result is a system of "institutional mimicry." The EU reproduces the trappings of national democracy—elections, party competition, and parliamentary procedures—to provide the appearance of accountability. Yet, because the [Lisbon Treaty](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:12012E) abandoned the goal of a common European identity in favor of a treaty-based association, the Union exercises federal authority without a corresponding federal *demos*. The architecture is functional. The legitimacy is formal.

## The Judicial Expansion of Competence

The tension between the Union's formal limits and its functional reality is rooted in the distinction between *Verbandskompetenz* (objective competence) and *Organkompetenz* (institutional power). While [Article 5 of the TEU](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:12012E) establishes the principle of conferral, the CJEU operates by interpreting the meaning and scope of those conferred powers. Rather than claiming new competences, the Court redefines the boundaries of existing ones.

This judicial function has become the primary driver of integration. Principles such as subsidiarity and proportionality, intended as hard legal barriers to expansion, are often treated as procedural checks. The result is a "competence confusion" where the boundary of EU power shifts without the need for formal treaty amendments. The legal framework provides the limit; the judicial interpretation provides the bypass.

## The Post-Functionalist Dilemma

The transition from a functional union to a legitimate federation is stalled by a structural contradiction in reform models. As analyzed by [Ben Crum of Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/B851EAF3D7FDB97EE1C13D2AB88460EE/S2045381723000102a.pdf/models-of-eu-constitutional-reform-what-do-we-learn-from-the-conference-on-the-future-of-europe.pdf), the EU faces a "post-functionalist dilemma": options that are politically viable for Member State governments typically lack democratic legitimacy, while those that are democratically legitimate are generally not politically viable.

The Conference on the Future of Europe (CoFoE) attempted to resolve this by introducing bottom-up citizen involvement, but the results highlight the gap between top-down viability and grassroots legitimacy. Reform continues to oscillate between intergovernmentalist models that preserve national sovereignty and supranational models that seek to empower the European Parliament. The system remains in a state of incrementalism. The political will for a true constitutional leap is absent.

## De-constitutionalization and Mimicry

The current democratic deficit is a qualitative failure of identity, not merely a mechanical problem of representation. This failure was codified in the [Lisbon Treaty](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:12012E), which represents a "de-constitutionalization" of the Union. By abandoning the goals of the 2005 Constitutional Treaty, the EU retreated from the aspiration of creating a common European identity or "constitutional patriotism," returning instead to a treaty-based association of states.

To mask this void, pro-EU actors have relied on "institutional mimicry." According to research from [ECPR](https://theloop.ecpr.eu/why-the-eus-democratic-deficit-persists/), the Union reproduces the trappings of national democracy—elections and party competition—under the assumption that citizen support will follow the form. This "trickle-down democracy" provides the appearance of accountability without the underlying political *demos* required for a genuine federation. The form is present. The substance is missing.

## The Legitimacy Paradox

The divergence between the Union's legal form and its functional power creates a systemic fragility. By operating as a federation through judicial interpretation rather than constitutional mandate, the EU avoids the political friction of treaty change but inherits a permanent legitimacy deficit. The pattern suggests that this "silent" expansion allows the center to govern effectively in the short term, but it leaves the entire architecture vulnerable to the first significant political shock that challenges the Court's authority. The choice is no longer between integration and sovereignty, but between a formalised federation and a fragile mimicry. The gap remains open.
