**The European Union possesses the world's largest single market on paper, yet its firms remain structurally fragmented because they cannot access the unified capital necessary to compete with US and Chinese giants.**

## The May 20 Mandate

Commission President Ursula von der Leyen called for the definitive completion of the Single Market in her address to the European Parliament on May 20. She specifically targeted "gold plating"—the tendency of member states to add national layers of complexity to EU rules [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/20/von-der-leyen-calls-for-completing-the-european-single-market-in-eu-parliament-address). The objective has shifted from simple market access to "scale-up capacity," with a new emphasis on a "digital by design" framework for semiconductors, cloud infrastructure, and artificial intelligence.

This push is a strategic pivot toward a "Buy European" industrial policy. The Commission frames the completion of the market as a geopolitical necessity, arguing that the transition to a clean economy remains vulnerable if Europe depends on external competitors for critical infrastructure.

Political will for this shift is uneven. Germany and the Nordics have expressed concerns that assertive "Buy European" measures risk retaliatory trade actions or economic isolation [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/feb/12/eu-leaders-clash-buy-european-belgium-summit). The result is a tiered compromise: European preference is currently debated as a tool for strategic assets in energy and defense, while open tenders remain for general goods. The architecture is in place. The integration is not.

## The Capital Markets Gap

The inability of European firms to scale is not a failure of ambition, but a consequence of a bank-based financial architecture that suppresses equity growth. For years, the Capital Markets Union (CMU) has existed as a "legislative ghost," where theoretical support from the European Council has not translated into adopted law [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/policy-brief/making-reality-europes-capital-markets-union). European household savings remain locked in real estate or low-yielding national assets rather than flowing into the high-growth firms the Single Market is designed to support.

The Commission is now pivoting toward a "Savings and Investment Union" (SIU). The structural logic involves expanding the powers of the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) and introducing an "SIU Scoreboard" to track national progress on pension and investment fund reforms. Access to certain EU funds may be conditioned on the implementation of these national capital-market reforms [ECIPE](https://ecipe.org/publications/boosting-competitiveness-deeper-better-cmu/). The tools for integration are being built. The willingness to use them remains conditional.

## The Industrialist-Free Trade Divide

The shift toward a "Buy European" policy has exposed a fault line between two competing visions of European sovereignty. France and Italy argue that strategic autonomy is impossible if Europe continues to outsource its critical security and energy transition needs to global competitors [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/feb/12/eu-leaders-clash-buy-european-belgium-summit). For these states, "European preference" in procurement is a tool to guarantee the survival of critical industrial bases.

Opposing this are the "free-trade" states, including Germany and the Nordic bloc, who view assertive industrial preference as a slide toward protectionism that risks economic isolation [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/feb/12/eu-leaders-clash-buy-european-belgium-summit). The current compromise is a tiered system: strict preference is reserved for "critical strategic assets" in energy and defense, with some proponents suggesting the use of WTO "security exceptions" to justify the deviation from open trade. The compromise manages the friction. It does not resolve the contradiction.

## The Systemic Failure of Integration

The "scale paradox" is evidenced by a persistent home bias that defies the logic of a single currency. ECB data reveals that frictions within the euro area have eased significantly less than those between the euro area and the United States—a decline of only 12 percentage points intra-euro compared to 25 percentage points euro-US [ECB](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/economic-bulletin/focus/2026/html/ecb.ebbox202603_01~a37dec2a44.en.html). This indicates that euro area investors find it structurally easier to hold US equities than equities from their own neighbors.

This financial fragmentation suggests that the barrier to scaling is institutional, not entrepreneurial. While the EU has harmonized laws to allow market access, it has not integrated the financial plumbing required to fund a company's growth from a national champion to a continental giant. The result is a market that is wide but shallow. Europe has the scale, but it lacks the depth.

## The Depth Problem

The shift toward a Savings and Investment Union acknowledges that a single market of consumers is ineffective without a single market of capital. However, the transition from a bank-led to a market-led financial system is not a technical adjustment; it is a political one. It requires member states to relinquish control over national pension regimes and accept a level of financial integration that alters the role of the national state in the economy. The tools for this transition are being designed, but the political will to implement them remains fragmented. Europe has the scale. It still lacks the depth.
