**The €14 billion STEP Scale-up scheme and proposed legislative frameworks mark a structural pivot from funding scientific excellence to building industrial capacity.**

## From Research to Market Entry

For decades, the European Union's approach to discovery followed the Horizon programs: a system designed to produce world-class research that frequently failed to survive the transition to commercialization. This "valley of death"—the window where deep-tech startups exhaust capital before achieving market scale—has left Europe structurally dependent on US and Chinese scaling models. The [European Innovation Council (EIC)](https://eic.ec.europa.eu/news/eight-european-startups-set-receive-scale-funding-eic-under-step-scale-scheme-2026-04-27_en) is now attempting to reverse this pattern through the STEP Scale-up scheme, providing equity investments of €10 million to €30 million per company to trigger private co-investments of up to €150 million.

This shift is legislative. Industry advocates, including Allied for Startups, have called for regulatory sandboxes and streamlined certification processes to reduce the time and cost of market entry. As Commissioner for Defence and Space Andrius Kubilius has noted, the EU must now "get down to business" on the space and deep-tech economy. The goal is to transform the Single Market from a passive trade zone into a coordinated launchpad for strategic technologies.

The necessity of this transition is evident in Europe's space autonomy. Despite the ambition of the IRIS² constellation, Europe remains constrained by industrial fragmentation and a reliance on SpaceX for critical missions. The [SWP Berlin](https://www.swp-berlin.org/publikation/european-autonomy-in-space) analysis indicates that the "geographic return" principle of the ESA continues to reinforce inefficiency, treating space capacity as a matter of national quotas rather than federal strategic necessity. Coordination is not the problem. The structure is.

## The Legislative Shift Toward Capacity

The transition from research to industrialization is being codified through the associated 2026-2027 Work Programme. The pattern suggests a priority of "strategic capacity" over "scientific excellence." Unlike the grant-centric models of previous Horizon cycles, the current framework focuses on the scaling phase. The [European Innovation Council (EIC)](https://eic.ec.europa.eu/news/eight-european-startups-set-receive-scale-funding-eic-under-step-scale-scheme-2026-04-27_en) implements this through the STEP Scale-up scheme, utilizing an equity model that provides investments between €10 million and €30 million per company. These injections are designed to trigger private co-investments of €50 million to €150 million, bridging the financing gap that typically halts deep-tech scaling.

Beyond capital, the legislative focus is shifting toward reducing market entry barriers. Proposals for regulatory sandboxes and streamlined certification processes aim to accelerate the time-to-market for high-CAPEX technologies in sectors like quantum computing and biotech. To reduce administrative friction, roughly half of all call budgets are moving toward lump-sum grants. The architecture for scaling is being built. Execution remains the primary variable.

## The Fragmentation of Space Autonomy

European discovery is most severely tested in the space sector, where a structural dependency on the United States persists. While the EU aims for independent launch and satellite capacity, operational reality is constrained by a clash between federal ambition and national industrial policy. The [SWP Berlin](https://www.swp-berlin.org/publikation/european-autonomy-in-space) analysis highlights that the ESA's "geographic return" principle—which allocates contracts based on member state contributions—reinforces inefficiency and prevents the creation of a unified federal market.

This fragmentation creates a critical capacity gap. For example, the IRIS² constellation targets 290 satellites by 2030; Starlink maintains a fleet of over 7,000. This disparity is political. Reliance on external providers for critical missions, including Galileo, leaves Europe vulnerable to the instrumentalisation of space data. As noted by [CSIS](https://csis.org/blogs/europe-corner/strategic-ambition-vs-structural-dependency-why-europe-cant-stand-alone-space), the rhetoric of strategic autonomy often masks a reality of industrial fragmentation and risk aversion. The capability gap is real.

## The Legacy of the Research-Led Model

The current pivot departs from a long-standing European precedent: the belief that scientific excellence would naturally translate into economic leadership. For decades, the EU's innovation strategy focused on the early stages of the research pipeline, producing world-class discoveries that US firms subsequently commercialized. This pattern established the "valley of death" as a permanent feature of the European landscape, where breakthroughs in the lab lacked the venture capital and regulatory agility to reach global scale.

This failure is now being addressed by treating the Single Market not as a trade zone, but as a coordinated launchpad. The shift toward "strategic capacity" in the 2026-2027 cycle acknowledges that discovery without the capacity to scale is a strategic liability. Efforts to integrate the entire value chain from research to market entry attempt to break this cycle. Europe is moving from a research-led to a business-led approach to deep-tech. The transition is structural.

## The Scaling Ceiling

The transition from a research-led to a capacity-led model is an admission that scientific excellence is not a proxy for strategic power. By integrating the value chain through the STEP scheme and proposed regulatory reforms, the EU is attempting to institutionalize the agility of the US venture model within a regulatory framework. However, the persistent friction in the space sector suggests that capital alone cannot solve the problem of industrial fragmentation. The real tension remains between the efficiency of a federal launchpad and the political comfort of national quotas. 

This is a question of governance, not funding. Acting as a collection of national interests, Europe can fund research. Acting as a federation, it can scale it. The choice between these two models is now a concrete policy decision.
