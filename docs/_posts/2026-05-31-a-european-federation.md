**The Union of European Federalists (UEF) has transitioned from advocating for gradual institutional reform to proposing a formal "Declaration of Independence and Sovereignty" for a federal European state.**

## The 2026-2030 Operational Roadmap

This shift was institutionalized on March 22, 2026, when the XXIX European Congress approved the [Strategic Plan 2026-2030](https://federalists.eu/resolutions-stat/strategic-plan-of-the-union-of-european-federalists-uef-2026-2030/). The UEF is deploying a "Federal Vanguard" strategy. The objective is to dismantle the requirement for unanimity that currently paralyzes the European Council, framing federalization as an operational necessity for competitiveness.

The [European Federal Manifesto 2026](https://federalists.eu/resolutions-stat/the-european-federal-manifesto-a-declaration-of-independence-and-sovereignty-2026/) identifies the current "confederation of nation-states" as a structural liability. Europe represents roughly 17.4% of global GDP but less than 5% of the world's population. The UEF argues that fragmented political agency is no longer viable. The strategy replaces the "equality between states" model with a system based on "equality between citizens," granting the European Parliament full legislative initiative and establishing a true bicameral system.

This approach runs parallel to EU enlargement. As MEP Sandro Gozi has noted, the Union cannot expand without simultaneous reform. To do so would be to institutionalize paralysis. The goal is a coordinated constitutional rupture—a move away from the top-down failures of the 2004 Constitutional Treaty toward a direct sovereign mandate.

## The Unanimity Constraint and the Citizen Mandate

The structural failure of the current European Council lies in the "veto trap"—the requirement for unanimity in key strategic areas that effectively grants any single member state a moratorium on collective action. The [Union of European Federalists (UEF)](https://federalists.eu/pr-reform-the-union-to-unify-europe/) argues that this intergovernmentalism is incompatible with a Union facing rapid enlargement. The proposed shift moves the basis of legitimacy from the "equality between states" to "equality between citizens."

This transition is operationalized through a move toward a true bicameral legislative system. By granting the European Parliament full legislative initiative and placing it on an equal footing with the Council, the "Federal Vanguard" strategy seeks to replace national mandates with a direct European sovereign mandate. The structural logic is clear: a federation governs by the will of its citizens, while a confederation is limited by the lowest common denominator of its members. The legal architecture for this shift exists in the aspiration for democratic accountability, but the political mechanism remains locked.

## The Mechanics of Differentiated Integration

The path to federalization is not a simultaneous leap for all twenty-seven members, but a strategic use of "differentiated integration." This is most evident in the proposal for a European Defence System. Under the [Gozi Report](https://federalists.eu/pr-reform-the-union-to-unify-europe/), the legal basis for a Common Defence is already present in the Treaties. Specifically, a qualified majority decision under Article 42(6) TEU and Protocol No.10 could enable the creation of a federal defense structure among willing Member States.

This approach allows a "Federal Vanguard" of states to bypass the unanimity requirement and build integrated capacity in real-time. It transforms the EU from a rigid bloc into a tiered system where integration is driven by operational necessity rather than diplomatic consensus. The goal is to project power and safeguard interests in a multipolar world where individual states lack the scale to compete. Coordination is a temporary bridge. Integration is the destination.

## The Legacy of the 2004 Constitutional Crisis

The current strategy responds to the failures of the 2004 Constitutional Treaty. That attempt at legal consolidation failed because it was a top-down, elite-driven project that relied on national ratifications and referendums, collapsing in France and the Netherlands in 2005. The 2004 crisis demonstrated that legal simplification is insufficient when it lacks geopolitical urgency or a grassroots mandate. It was a project of administration, not a project of strategic autonomy.

In contrast, the 2026 approach frames federalization as a "Declaration of Independence and Sovereignty." It shifts the argument from legal tidiness to geopolitical necessity, positioning the federal state as the only viable response to the asymmetry between Europe's economic weight and its political agency. While the 2004 attempt sought to refine the existing system, the "Federal Vanguard" proposes a constitutional rupture. The lesson of 2004 is that the veto cannot be managed; it must be dismantled.

## The Sovereignty Tension

The "Federal Vanguard" strategy accepts a fundamental truth: the transition to a federal state cannot be achieved through the consensus of the very states that benefit from the current intergovernmental deadlock. While the legal pathways for differentiated integration exist, they require a political will that operates outside the unanimity trap. The structural tension now lies between the ability to coordinate among a willing minority and the necessity of a total constitutional rupture to achieve true sovereignty. Europe can continue to refine its administrative layers, or it can choose to govern. The difference is a matter of political agency.
