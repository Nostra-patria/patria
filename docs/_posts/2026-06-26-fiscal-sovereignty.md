**The OECD agreement on a 15% global minimum tax (Pillar 2) neutralizes the competitive advantage of ultra-low-tax jurisdictions. This removes the primary structural barrier to a centralized European tax authority.**

## The Transition from Own Resources

The European Union operates on a contribution-based "Own Resources" system. Member states transfer funds to a common pot based on GNI and VAT percentages. This model creates budgetary dependency; the Union cannot act independently of member-state interests because it lacks tax ownership—the power to define the tax base and set rates. As noted by Giulia Rossolillo, this dependency forces the EU into a "unanimity trap," where any adjustment to the revenue stream requires the consent of every member state, often leading to systemic deadlock [The Federalist](https://www.thefederalist.eu/site/index.php/en/essays/2619-from-own-resources-to-fiscal-union).

The European Commission's 2026 Tax Outlook highlights that while the technical framework for coordination exists, member-state reluctance remains the primary obstacle to harmonized corporate tax floors. This reluctance is structural. For mid-sized states, tax policy is the primary tool for attracting mobile capital in the absence of superior infrastructure or scale. In this zero-sum game, surrendering tax sovereignty is viewed as a loss of national competitiveness [European Review](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/european-review/article/bargaining-the-euro-making-an-eu-fiscal-union-politically-acceptable/9C6AB56492422E6046BB40FF9AC1076A).

The implementation of Pillar 2 changes the calculus. Because any effective tax rate below 15% now triggers a "top-up tax" from other jurisdictions, the incentive for a race-to-the-bottom has vanished. Competition has shifted from blanket rates to specific, transparent incentives for R&D and green energy. This shift provides a technical bridge for the Union to move from resource pooling to tax sharing. The center could claim revenue directly and perform sovereign functions, such as counter-cyclical fiscal stabilization, without relying on national guarantees [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/analysis/how-global-minimum-tax-amendments-could-reshape-europes-tax-incentives).

## The Tax Ownership Gap

The "Own Resources" system functions as a resource-pooling mechanism rather than a sovereign fiscal authority. Without the legal power to define a tax base or set rates, the Union cannot generate revenue independently of member-state transfers.

This lack of autonomy creates a systemic vulnerability. Because changes to the revenue stream typically require the consent of every member state, the Union cannot act decisively during fiscal crises. As Nico Groenendijk notes, achieving federal fiscal capacity requires moving toward a tax-sharing model where the center has a direct claim on revenue [Revenue Capacity of the EU](https://www.cogitatiopress.com/politicsandgovernance/article/download/7240/3481).

Without this shift, the EU cannot perform basic sovereign functions. It cannot implement counter-cyclical fiscal stabilization or borrow against its own future revenue without seeking member-state guarantees. The architecture for coordination exists. The legal authority to execute it does not.

## The Zero-Sum Game of National Competition

For mid-sized member states, the transition to a fiscal union is often viewed as a zero-sum game. In the absence of the massive scale of Germany or the specialized infrastructure of the Benelux, these states rely on tax levers to attract investment. Surrendering the power to set corporate tax rates is seen not as a move toward efficiency, but as a loss of the only competitive lever they possess.

This strategic interaction extends to the interpretation of fiscal rules. Member states frequently utilize "defence escape hatches" within the Stability and Growth Pact to maintain national advantages while appearing compliant with EU-level recommendations. The European Commission and the Council set the framework, but national treasuries respond with budgets designed to maximize local utility. The result is a fragmented landscape where fiscal surveillance is a negotiation rather than an enforcement mechanism [CEPR/VoxEU](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/eus-new-fiscal-rules-first-gaps-between-hopes-and-outcomes).

The strongest argument for maintaining this fragmentation is the preservation of democratic accountability. Critics argue that shifting the power to tax and spend from national parliaments to EU institutions decouples authority from electoral mandates. If a centralized fiscal union removes the ability of a national government to prioritize its own citizens' needs through tax breaks, the result is a democratic deficit that administrative efficiency cannot offset.

## The Cost of Non-Europe

The economic burden of this fragmentation is termed the "cost of non-Europe" (CoNE). In taxation, this manifests as a thicket of diverging rules for wealth, cryptoassets, and general compliance. These discrepancies facilitate aggressive tax planning and erode the national tax bases that member states fight to protect. The EPRS reports that such differences in design and enforcement weaken the single market and limit fair competition [EPRS](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/STUD/2025/774671/EPRS_STU(2025)774671_EN.pdf).

Historically, the EU attempted to resolve this through incremental harmonization. These efforts consistently stalled against the requirement for unanimity. The "race-to-the-bottom" incentive—where states lowered rates to outcompete neighbors—rendered any centralized corporate tax base politically impossible.

The 15% global minimum tax under Pillar 2 breaks this historical cycle. By ensuring that any rate below 15% is topped up by other jurisdictions, the OECD agreement removes the primary reward for ultra-low-tax competition. The competitive advantage of the tax haven is gone. This provides a viable path toward a common corporate tax base, as the political cost of surrender is now lower than the economic cost of remaining fragmented.

## The Institutional Deadlock

The implementation of Pillar 2 removes the technical excuse for fragmentation, but it does not solve the political arithmetic of sovereignty. A transition to a true fiscal union requires a treaty change to allow the Union to levy taxes directly. This shift would move the EU from a system of resource pooling to one of tax sharing, altering the relationship between national treasuries and the center.

The risk of a democratic deficit remains the primary friction point, as the power to tax is the most direct expression of political accountability. Whether the Union can bridge this gap depends on whether member states view a centralized fiscal authority as a loss of autonomy or as the only way to protect their tax bases from global erosion. 

The technical bridge is ready. The political will is not.
