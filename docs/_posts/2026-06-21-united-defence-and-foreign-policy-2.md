**Seventeen EU member states have activated the "National Escape Clause" (NEC) to bypass defence spending targets, institutionalizing fiscal fragmentation while the bloc pursues integrated strategic autonomy.**

## The Institutionalization of the Opt-Out

Reports from the Council of the EU confirm the activation of the National Escape Clause—a mechanism introduced in the "Readiness 2030" package proposed by the European Commission in March 2025. The clause allows member states to increase spending on military equipment, infrastructure, and personnel without violating EU fiscal rules, providing "additional budgetary space" [Consilium](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/national-escape-clause-for-defence-expenditure-nec/). While presented as a flexibility tool, the mechanism permits individual capitals to prioritize national budgetary control over the harmonized fiscal commitment required for a unified European defence architecture.

This widespread activation creates a structural contradiction. The EU seeks a cohesive "Defence Readiness" posture, yet it provides a legal pathway for member states to opt-out of the fiscal discipline necessary to sustain it. The pattern suggests that for a majority of the bloc, short-term budgetary flexibility outweighs the long-term stability of a European defence union.

## The Fiscal Safety Valve

The National Escape Clause operates as a political safety valve. It acknowledges a fundamental structural deficiency: the EU lacks a federal fiscal authority for defence. A centralized budget would trigger a sovereignty shock that most member states currently reject.

The resulting architecture relies on a three-pillar system. NATO provides the command and control structure; the EU manages funding and pan-European solidarity; and minilateral arrangements handle fast-track capability development [ECFR](https://ecfr.eu/wp-contentcontent/uploads/2026/06/Making-defence-European-again.pdf). The NEC allows capitals to maintain the appearance of integrated readiness while preserving domestic fiscal control. The coordination exists in name. The authority remains national.

## The Divergence of Capability and Budget

A sharp divide has emerged between high-end strategic integration and broad fiscal discipline. While 17 states utilize the NEC to bypass spending targets, a core group of "power users" pursues a separate path of harmonization. On February 12, 2026, France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Sweden, and the United Kingdom signed a Letter of Intent to accelerate the development of "long-range one-way effectors," including airborne early warning and low-cost 500km+ capabilities [Joint Communiqué](https://www.gov.pl/web/national-defence/joint-communiqu-by-the-defence-ministries-of-elsa-nations).

This creates a two-speed security architecture. The ELSA nations pursue integrated high-end procurement, while the broader bloc manages its commitments through legal loopholes. The strategic cost is a loss of deterrence credibility. When member states prioritize short-term budgetary flexibility—such as Spain capping its budget at "just over 2%" to maintain social spending—they undermine the structural stability of the European defence union. The EU is trading long-term coherence for immediate political stability.

## The Legacy of Quantitative Targets

The current reliance on the NEC results from a decade of failed quantitative spending targets. For years, the EU and NATO focused on the 2% GDP benchmark, a metric that prioritized the amount of money spent over how it was spent. This approach failed to prevent systemic redundancies because European militaries were designed to interface with the United States, not with each other. Even the 2025 shift toward a 5% GDP target, driven by U.S. political pressure, did not solve the underlying coordination gap.

Historical benchmarks illustrate this friction. The European Defence Agency set a target for collaborative investment at 35% in 2007, yet reports suggest overall EU joint investment remained at only 18% in 2022 [EPRS](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/thinkin/en/document/EPRS_PM2024-0123-FACTSHEET_EN). While the "Defence Readiness Roadmap 2030" has reportedly raised this ambition to 40% by 2027, the NEC proves that the political will to enforce such a target is absent. Europe is attempting to build a security architecture that functions without U.S. participation while relying on the fiscal habits of the era of outsourcing.

## The Cost of Budgetary Flexibility

The National Escape Clause institutionalizes a system where the EU defines its strategic goals but allows its members to decide the funding. This arrangement prevents the "sovereignty shock" of a centralized budget, but it ensures that European deterrence remains a collection of national efforts rather than a unified capability. The distance between the "power users" of the ELSA track and the 17 states utilizing the NEC creates a fragmented security architecture. The bloc now faces a concrete choice: accept a permanent two-speed defense or establish a federal fiscal authority. Coordination has a ceiling.
