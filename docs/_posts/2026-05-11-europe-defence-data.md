**Europe's defence problem is not a shortage of soldiers. It is a surplus of flags.**

EU member states combined field more active-duty military personnel than the United States. They collectively outspend Russia on defence every year. Yet on the one question that matters — can Europe act as one military force without American permission? — the answer remains: no.

## What is being said

A widely repeated narrative, amplified following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine and renewed tensions over NATO burden-sharing, holds that Europe is militarily helpless. The claim is used to justify both continued NATO dependence and — by critics — European strategic ambivalence.

The framing is not wrong. But it obscures where the actual problem lies.

## What the evidence shows

EU member states collectively spent approximately €326 billion on defence in 2024, according to the [European Defence Agency](https://www.eda.europa.eu/). That figure exceeds Russia's entire GDP-adjusted defence budget. Combined active military personnel across EU members exceeds 1.4 million — more than the US active duty force of approximately 1.3 million.

The problem is fragmentation. Those 1.4 million soldiers operate under [27 separate chains of command](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/en/press-room/), 17 different tank models, and defence procurement processes that remain overwhelmingly national. The [RAND Corporation estimated in 2023](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA2133-1.html) that EU defence fragmentation costs Europe between €25–100 billion annually in duplication, incompatibility, and scale inefficiency.

PESCO — the EU's Permanent Structured Cooperation on defence — has launched 67 collaborative projects since 2017. Progress has been real but slow, and the projects remain voluntary.

## European context

The structural problem is not money or manpower. It is sovereignty: every European government that has pooled defence with a neighbour has implicitly accepted that its soldiers might die under someone else's political decision. That is a decision no national parliament can make unilaterally — and under the current EU treaty architecture, no federal authority exists to make it democratically.

This is the core argument for European federation that cannot be resolved by deeper intergovernmental cooperation. Coordination has a ceiling. Shared command requires shared democratic legitimacy.

A European federation with a unified defence structure would not need more soldiers. It would need fewer flags.
