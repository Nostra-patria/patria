**The OECD Better Life Index tracks eleven dimensions of prosperity, yet the European Union continues to govern its budget and policy priorities through the raw lens of GDP. This structural disconnect ensures that 'well-being' remains a statistical exercise rather than a governing principle.**

## The Ambition of the Beyond GDP Framework

The European Commission and the Joint Research Centre (JRC) are operationalizing a "Beyond GDP" framework to replace aggregate economic growth with a multi-dimensional dashboard of inclusive prosperity. This shift relies on the [OECD Better Life Index](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/tools/well-being-data-monitor/better-life-index.html), which monitors indicators from civic engagement to work-life balance to reflect the material conditions of citizens. The objective is a model where environmental sustainability and falling inequality serve as the primary metrics of success.

The transition is primarily diagnostic. While the [Council document ST-7107-2026-INIT](https://data.consilium.europa.eu/doc/document/ST-7107-2026-INIT/en/pdf) outlines a "holistic approach" to child well-being and social fairness, these conclusions are political agreements rather than binding legislative acts. Article 24 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights applies to Member States only when implementing EU law, leaving the delivery of social fairness to decentralized national targets.

The result is a governance gap. The EU can now measure the failure of a human-centered economy with high precision, but it lacks the legal mandate to enforce the remedy.

## The Housing Competence Gap

The European Union's approach to affordable housing illustrates the tension between framing a service as a "fundamental right" and the legal reality of national competence. Through the [European Affordable Housing Plan](https://housing.ec.europa.eu/european-affordable-housing-plan_en), the Commission has integrated housing objectives into National Recovery and Resilience Plans (NRRPs). This mechanism uses the [Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF)](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/thinktank/en/document/EPRS_BRI(2026)785695) to support 50 housing-related investments and 21 reforms across Member States.

To bypass bureaucratic delays, the Commission revised State aid rules, allowing Member States to support social housing projects without prior notification or authorization. This is a strategic attempt to use financial leverage where legislative authority is absent. The RRF provides the capital, but the implementation remains decentralized. The structural gap persists.

## National Divergence and the WEGo Model

The implementation of "well-being" varies between the EU's top-down coordination and the systemic transformations seen in the Wellbeing Economy Governments (WEGo) partnership. While the EU focuses on the [coordination of social security systems](https://employment-social-affairs.ec.europa.eu/policies-and-activities/moving-working-europe/eu-social-security-coordination_en) to ensure benefit portability for mobile citizens, WEGo members like Finland and Scotland are redesigning the economic objective itself.

Finland has moved beyond benefit coordination to implement an "Economy of Wellbeing," featuring a Universal Basic Income (UBI) pilot and a focus on the social determinants of health. This represents a shift from managing the symptoms of a market economy to altering its foundational logic. The EU's current framework ensures a citizen does not lose coverage when crossing a border, but it does not challenge why the coverage is insufficient. One is a logistical success; the other is a structural ambition.

## The Precedent of Non-Binding Guidelines

The current effort to prioritize child well-being follows a long-standing EU pattern of using non-binding "Conclusions" to signal political direction without creating legal obligations. [Council document ST-7107-2026-INIT](https://data.consilium.europa.eu/doc/document/ST-7107-2026-INIT/en/pdf) exemplifies this approach, relying on the European Pillar of Social Rights and the European Child Guarantee. These are political commitments, not binding legislative acts.

Under this framework, the EU sets headline targets—such as reducing the number of people at risk of poverty by 15 million by 2030—but leaves the actual targets to the discretion of individual Member States. This mirrors previous attempts to harmonize social standards where the Union provides the diagnostic tools and the targets, but not the enforcement mechanism. The architecture for measurement is complete. The mandate for delivery is not.

## The Institutional Threshold

The shift toward a well-being economy remains a diagnostic achievement rather than a governing one. The EU has developed the metrics to identify the failure of the current model, but it continues to rely on non-binding conclusions and financial leverage to influence areas of national competence. 

Bridging this gap would require moving beyond the coordination of existing benefits toward a shared legal framework for social rights. This suggests a transition from a logistical union to a social one. The architecture for measurement is ready. The political will for enforcement is not.
