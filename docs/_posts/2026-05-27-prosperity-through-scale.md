**European later-stage venture capital investment is reported at $21.3 billion, a fraction of the $133 billion deployed in the United States. This disparity is not a failure of entrepreneurship, but a structural consequence of a fragmented capital market that cannot provide the large investment tickets required for scaling.**

## The Shift Toward a Master Regulation

The European Commission is attempting to break this liquidity trap by removing the "interpretative latitude" of national supervisors. On December 4, 2025, the Commission issued a market integration package to replace flexible directives with a uniform regulatory framework. The center-piece is the "Master Regulation," which amends Directives 2009/65/EC, 2011/61/EU, and 2014/65/EU to ensure that fund management firms operating across borders face the same rules in Paris as they do in Warsaw [European Parliamentary Research Service](https://epthinktank.eu/2026/02/18/capital-markets-integration-and-supervision-master-regulation-eu-legislation-in-progress/).

A coalition of the "big six" economies—France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Poland, and Spain—backs this legislative push. In a joint letter to economy chief Valdis Dombrovskis, these states labeled the integration of capital markets an "urgent necessity" [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/03/12/eus-largest-economies-push-for-faster-capitals-market-integration-in-joint-letter). The group seeks a formal agreement on the Market Integration and Supervision Package (MISP) by June 2026, potentially utilizing "enhanced cooperation" to bypass the unanimity requirements that have historically stalled the Capital Markets Union.

Coordination has a structural ceiling. The goal is now uniformity.

## The Equity Gap and the Scaleup Fund

The disparity between European and US capital markets is most acute at the later stages of company growth. While early-stage funding is relatively abundant, EU scaleups frequently struggle to secure funding rounds exceeding €100 million—a threshold noted in structural analyses of the equity gap—where US venture capital investment dwarfs the European equivalent [Delors Centre Policy Brief](https://www.sciencespo.fr/ecole-affaires-publiques/sites/sciencespo.fr.ecole-affaires-publiques/files/BONGIORNO-Ruggero.pdf). This gap is not a product of missing talent, but a structural failure of fund size. The pattern suggests that very few European VC funds exceed the €1 billion mark, meaning they cannot provide the large investment tickets necessary for a company to scale globally without fleeing to US markets [Delors Centre Policy Brief](https://www.sciencespo.fr/ecole-affaires-publiques/sites/sciencespo.fr.ecole-affaires-publiques/files/BONGIORNO-Ruggero.pdf).

The Scaleup Europe Fund is the Commission's attempt to bridge this liquidity trap. According to EQT Group, the fund targets high-value, later-stage rounds, utilizing a mix of public European Innovation Council (EIC) capital and private investment [EQT Group](https://eqtgroup.com/news/eqt-selected-to-lead-the-scaleup-europe-fund-2026-05-18). To prevent further capital flight, the EU is pairing this fund with a proposed pan-European corporate legal framework and a Deep Tech Stock Exchange.

The capital is being deployed. The structural vehicle for its long-term growth remains under construction.

## The Sovereignty Paradox of "Buy European"

Parallel to the effort to integrate capital, the European Commission is pursuing the "One Europe, One Market" action plan, centered on a "Buy European" strategy. This policy redirects public procurement toward EU-based production in strategic sectors such as defence, clean tech, chips, and chemicals [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/02/26/the-eu-wants-to-buy-european-but-can-it). The logic is grounded in strategic autonomy: as Guntram Wolff of Bruegel notes, autonomy requires that technology be made in Europe to avoid dependencies on external powers [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org).

This creates a tension between strategic industrial policy and the principles of a non-protectionist Single Market. While there is a broad consensus on "hard security" and defence as legitimate grounds for "Buy European" rules to reduce US geopolitical leverage, applying the same logic to chemicals or automotive sectors risks a slide into protectionism. This friction is concrete; a proposal expected before Christmas was withdrawn because member states could not agree on where the line between security and protectionism should be drawn [FT](https://www.ft.com/content/b92a4c09-a12b-4f9e-ad22-c541c1ad7e9f).

Strategic autonomy requires scale. Scale cannot be achieved through protectionism alone.

## Member State Dynamics and the "Big Six"

The push for the Market Integration and Supervision Package (MISP) is driven by a core coalition of the "big six" economies: France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Poland, and Spain. This group has moved beyond diplomatic suggestions, sending a joint letter to economy chief Valdis Dombrovskis and the Eurogroup President to label capital market integration an "urgent necessity" [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/03/12/eus-largest-economies-push-for-faster-capitals-market-integration-in-joint-letter).

The objective is to secure a formal agreement on the MISP by June 2026. To achieve this, they are advocating for "enhanced cooperation," a mechanism that allows a subset of member states to move forward with integration when the required unanimity of all 27 states cannot be reached. This axis of integration is also pushing for pan-European private digital payment networks to challenge the dominance of US providers like Visa and Mastercard, who have historically dominated cross-border payments [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/eu-eyes-plan-deepen-single-market-march-accelerate-capital-markets-union-2026-02-12/).

The "big six" are creating a core of integration. The remaining states remain the perimeter of fragmentation.

## The Regulatory Ceiling

The transition from flexible directives to the Master Regulation suggests that the EU has reached the limit of voluntary coordination. By shifting to a uniform federal framework, the "big six" are acknowledging that national supervisory latitude is not a feature of the Single Market, but a structural barrier to it. However, the use of "enhanced cooperation" to bypass unanimity creates a two-speed capital market, where a core of integrated economies operates under a different logic than the perimeter.

The architecture is ready. The political will is not. The choice between a unified federal market and a fragmented collection of national silos is now a concrete policy question, not a philosophical one.
