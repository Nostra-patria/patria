**The Union of European Federalists has published the European Federal Manifesto 2026, calling for a "Declaration of Independence and Sovereignty" to replace the current treaty-based EU system with a supreme federal legal order.**

## The Manifesto and the Sovereign Break

The [European Federal Manifesto 2026](https://federalists.eu/resolutions-stat/the-european-federal-manifesto-a-declaration-of-independence-and-sovereignty-2026/) argues that the existing intergovernmental coordination of the European Union no longer functions as a viable mechanism for competitiveness in a global order. The document proposes a structural shift: the transition from a union of states to a genuine federation. This "sovereign break" seeks to dismantle the unanimity rule in foreign policy and defense, which the manifesto describes as a "cage of national vetoes" that renders strategic autonomy theoretical.

Under this proposed architecture, a "vanguard group" of willing Member States would initiate the transformation by establishing a European Federal Constitution. This new legal order would endow supranational institutions with sovereign powers, positioning the European Parliament as the central pillar of democratic legitimacy with the right of legislative initiative. The European Commission would transition into the executive authority of the federation, led by a President accountable to the Parliament.

The federalists frame this move as a strategic necessity. The structural logic suggests that without a formal federal state, Europe remains a fragmented entity, vulnerable to the strategic interests of the United States, China, or Russia. The choice is binary: a Federal Union or historical irrelevance.

## The Constitutional Division of Power

The proposed transition replaces treaty-based coordination with a supreme European Federal Constitution. This architecture establishes a strict separation of competencies to resolve the tension between federal efficiency and regional autonomy. The federal level would exercise sovereign control over foreign policy, defense, trade, welfare, and fundamental rights, while reserving education, housing, and cultural policy for national or regional administrations [Together for Europe](https://togetherforeurope.com/the-democratic-case-for-european-federation/).

This model dismantles the "intergovernmental oligarchy" currently represented by the Council. By shifting the Union from a technocratic administration to a political entity, the framework ensures that the European Commission evolves into the federation's executive authority, led by a President elected based on European election results and directly accountable to the Parliament. Institutional clarity replaces ambiguity.

## The Vanguard Strategy and Member State Friction

The "sovereign break" does not assume a simultaneous leap by all twenty-seven members. Instead, the [Union of European Federalists](https://federalists.eu/is-a-federal-european-union-possible-yes-but-not-at-twenty-seven/) advocates for a "vanguard group" of willing states to advance deeper integration in strategic areas, specifically defense and fiscal capacity. This multi-speed transition allows a core group to bypass the "cage of national vetoes" while remaining anchored within the broader EU institutional structure.

This approach creates a divide between federalist states and those prioritizing national sovereignty. The primary risk is the creation of a tiered Europe, where a sovereign core alienates non-federalist members and fractures the Union's cohesion. However, proponents argue that the current unanimity rule in foreign policy is a structural failure that prevents the Union from acting as a single entity. Coordination has a ceiling. The vanguard strategy is an attempt to break through it.

## The Legitimacy Gap and Historical Precedent

The push for a formal constitution responds to the failure of previous elite-led attempts at integration. The [SWP](https://www.swp-berlin.org/en/publication/eu-failed-constitutional-referenda/) notes that the 2005 referenda in France and the Netherlands failed largely due to a lack of genuine politicization and a disconnect between the drafting elites and the general public. These failures entrenched a preference for "silent revolutions"—incremental, administrative updates to treaties that avoid public scrutiny.

The 2026 Manifesto rejects this incrementalism. The pattern suggests that the legitimacy gap cannot be closed through more efficient administration, but only by entrenching the EU as a transparent, democratic project. The current democratic deficit in external relations—where the Council often operates with limited oversight and utilizes "mixed agreements" to bypass scrutiny [European Papers](https://www.europeanpapers.eu/e-journal/democracy-borders-analyzing-role-democratic-principles-eu-external-relations-law)—serves as the evidence for this shift. Administrative integration has reached its limit.

## The Limit of Treaty-Based Coordination

The proposal for a sovereign federal break acknowledges that the current EU architecture cannot be patched through incremental treaty updates. By shifting the debate from administrative efficiency to a formal division of sovereign competencies, the Manifesto forces a confrontation with the nation-state veto. 

A vanguard group of states could bypass the unanimity rule, but this creates a permanent structural tension between a federal core and a treaty-based periphery. The project no longer asks if integration is possible, but whether the Union can survive its own fragmentation. The choice is now structural.
