**The mobilization of frozen Russian assets to fund a €90bn loan for Ukraine proves that the European Union can act as a decisive financial entity; it cannot yet act as a decisive strategic one.**

## The Unanimity Constraint

The EU's ability to project power is decoupled from its financial capacity. While the Union coordinates massive fiscal instruments, such as the [€90bn loan mechanism for Ukraine](https://epthinktank.eu/2026/02/09/eu-support-for-ukraine-for-2026-2027/), its Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP) remains tethered to the unanimity rule. This requirement ensures that a single member state can veto strategic moves. The result is a foreign policy that searches for the lowest common denominator. The Union possesses the wealth of a superpower but the decision-making speed of a committee.

To resolve this structural paralysis, a proposal for a Permanent EU Security Council (ESC) is gaining traction. Envisioned as a body of 10–12 member states—utilizing rotating seats or permanent positions for the largest economies—the ESC would have the authority to make binding decisions on foreign and defence policy [EUInsider](https://www.euinsider.eu/news/eu-security-council-proposal-unanimity-2026). The objective is to move beyond financial solidarity and toward institutional sovereignty.

The "Weimar-plus" axis of Germany, France, and Poland leads this push, advocating for a more integrated defense industrial base and a streamlined approach to Ukraine's accession. However, the transition is not merely technical. For smaller member states in Central and Northern Europe, the unanimity rule is not a bug but a primary lever of influence. Removing it challenges the structural logic of intergovernmental decision-making that has defined the Union for decades.

## The Institutional Mechanism

The proposal for a Permanent EU Security Council seeks to move the Union from a coordination hub to a governing body. By utilizing a body of 10–12 member states with the authority to make binding decisions on foreign and defence policy [EUInsider](https://www.euinsider.eu/news/eu-security-council-proposal-unanimity-2026), the EU could react to crises at a speed currently impossible under the CFSP framework.

To avoid the political gridlock of formal treaty change, proponents are exploring operational paths. These include "Enhanced Cooperation," which allows a subset of members to integrate further, or the creation of binding intergovernmental agreements outside the formal treaty structure. Commissioner Andrius Kubilius has advocated for a European Defence Union to create a streamlined political alignment and command structure. The goal is to create a legal bridge between financial capacity and strategic action. The architecture for cooperation exists. The legal mandate for command does not.

## The Weimar-Plus Axis and the Leverage Gap

The drive toward this institutional shift centers on the "Weimar-plus" axis of Germany, France, and Poland. This core group is pushing for an aggressive integration of Ukraine into the European defense technological and industrial base and a coordinated enforcement of sanctions against Russia's "shadow fleet" in the Baltic Sea [Defense News](https://www.defensenews.com/global/europe/2025/09/30/germany-france-poland-push-for-closer-eu-ukraine-defense-ties/). For these states, the current unanimity rule is a strategic liability.

Resistance is rooted in the structural logic of the Union's current power dynamics. Smaller member states in Central and Northern Europe view the unanimity rule as their primary lever of influence over EU foreign policy [EUInsider](https://www.euinsider.eu/news/eu-security-council-proposal-unanimity-2026). In their view, a concentrated security council would permanently sideline smaller capitals in favor of the largest economies. This creates a tension between the need for strategic speed and the desire for institutional equity. The conflict is not about the goal of security, but about who holds the veto.

## Financial Solidarity versus Strategic Projection

The €90bn loan mechanism for Ukraine, backed by revenues from frozen Russian central bank assets, serves as a proof-of-concept. It demonstrates that the EU can achieve operational coordination when the mechanism is purely fiscal [Epthinktank](https://epthinktank.eu/2026/02/09/eu-support-for-ukraine-for-2026-2027/). However, as noted by Bruegel, this financial success has not translated into strategic projection. The Union has yet to overcome the collective-action problems associated with pooling military responsibilities and financing common defence [Bruegel](https://www.bruegel.org/working-paper/european-union-and-war-ukraine-more-money-not-more-europe).

The pattern suggests a fundamental asymmetry: the EU is a superpower in finance but remains a collection of 27 actors in security. This internal fragmentation also undermines the EU's goals at the United Nations. While the EU provides roughly 22% of the UN regular budget and 21% of peacekeeping funding, its diplomatic voice on the UN Security Council remains fragmented [Security Council Report](https://www.securitycouncilreport.org/monthly-forecast/2026-04/eu-un-cooperation-3.php). Consolidating its security voice internally through an ESC is the prerequisite for translating financial contributions into strategic leverage. The money is there. The authority is not.

## The Limit of the Current Model

The proposal for a Permanent EU Security Council is an attempt to align the Union's political authority with its financial scale. However, the transition from a coordination hub to a governing body requires more than a new committee; it requires a shift in how member states perceive sovereignty. If the Union continues to rely on "Enhanced Cooperation" or parallel intergovernmental arrangements, it may gain speed, but it will struggle with legitimacy. The choice is between a fragile unanimity and a tiered security architecture. The friction is now structural.
