# The Armenian Pivot: Can Europe Actually Project Power?

**This week, the streets of Yerevan are filled with the presence of European leaders. The summits are framed as a gesture of solidarity and a strategic alignment with a partner seeking to diversify its security dependencies. But beyond the photo opportunities and the diplomatic communiqués, a fundamental question remains: is the European Union actually capable of projecting power, or is it merely projecting an image of power?**

## Beyond the Symbolism

For decades, the EU has operated as a 'normative power'—a giant that influences the world through trade, regulations, and the promotion of democratic values. This model worked during the era of undisputed Western hegemony. But in the current age of hard geopolitics, normative power is insufficient.

The convergence on Yerevan is a strategic necessity. Armenia, currently navigating the precarious space between a declining Russian security guarantee and a desire for Western integration, is the perfect litmus test for the EU's 'Strategic Autonomy.' If Europe can provide the stability and security necessary for Armenia to pivot away from Moscow, it proves that the EU is no longer just a trade bloc, but a geopolitical actor.

However, the danger is that the EU treats this as another 'mission of support.' Providing observers and financial aid is not the same as providing security. True strategic autonomy in the East requires the EU to offer concrete, reliable security guarantees—capabilities that it currently lacks because it has spent decades outsourcing its hard power to the United States.

## The US Shadow and the European Gap

The tragedy of European foreign policy is that it has always been a satellite of US strategy. When the US is engaged, Europe follows. When the US retreats, Europe panics. 

The 'Armenian Pivot' is only meaningful if it is a *European* pivot. If the EU is simply acting as a conduit for US interests or waiting for Washington's green light to act, then the summits in Yerevan are not an exercise in autonomy, but an exercise in coordination. 

The gap is clear: Europe has the economic weight and the diplomatic legitimacy to lead in the Caucasus, but it lacks the military and strategic coherence to back that leadership with force if necessary. To be a global player, Europe must stop asking 'what does the US want?' and start asking 'what does Europe need?'

## Analysis: From Normative Power to Security Provider

Patria's view: The summits in Armenia are a symptom of a larger realization: the EU cannot afford to be just a 'civilian power' in a world of tanks and missiles.

To move from projecting an image of power to actually projecting power, the EU must implement three strategic shifts:

1. **Security-First Diplomacy**: Trade and aid must be secondary to security guarantees. A partner will not pivot toward Europe if they believe Europe cannot protect them from a neighbor's aggression.
2. **Strategic Coherence**: The EU must stop the fragmentation of its foreign policy. A 'European' approach to the Caucasus must be a single, coherent strategy, not a collection of Member State preferences.
3. **Hard Power Integration**: Strategic autonomy is a lie if it doesn't include the ability to deploy force. The EU must accelerate the integration of its defence capabilities to ensure that its diplomatic promises are backed by credible deterrents.

## Conclusion

Yerevan is not just a city in the Caucasus; it is a laboratory for the future of the European Union. If the EU succeeds in stabilizing Armenia and creating a viable alternative to Russian influence, it will be the first real proof that Strategic Autonomy is more than a buzzword.

If it fails, it will be a stark reminder that without a unified defence and a clear-eyed will to act, Europe remains a passenger in a geopolitical game played by others. The world is watching Yerevan. It is time for Europe to show that it can do more than just attend a summit.
