# The Security-Sovereignty Gap: Why 'Defence Readiness' is a Dangerous Illusion

**This week, EU defence ministers gather to discuss 'defence readiness' and the continuing military support for Ukraine. On the surface, the agenda is a sign of a waking giant—a Europe finally acknowledging that its security cannot be outsourced. But look closer, and the pattern remains unchanged. We are witnessing the same intergovernmental dance we have performed for decades: a series of coordinated reactions masquerading as a strategic vision.**

## The Readiness Trap

The term 'defence readiness' is a carefully chosen piece of diplomatic phrasing. It implies that the tools for security already exist, and we simply need to ensure they are 'ready' for use. This is a fallacy. 

True readiness in the 21st century is not about whether a national army can deploy its tanks within 72 hours; it is about whether a continent can act as a single strategic entity. Currently, the EU is not a strategic entity; it is a diplomatic club of 27 different security architectures, some of which are deeply integrated into NATO, others of which are struggling to maintain basic deterrence capabilities.

By focusing on 'readiness' rather than 'transformation,' the EU is merely polishing the relics of a national-centric defence model. We are discussing how to better support Ukraine and how to monitor the Middle East, yet we still lack a unified European command structure that can move beyond the 'consensus' model of decision-making, which is effectively a veto for the most reluctant member.

## The Intergovernmental Deadlock

The fundamental problem is that the EU continues to treat defence as a matter of national sovereignty. In our current geopolitical climate, this is a contradiction in terms. You cannot have national security in a world where the threats are continental and the solutions are systemic.

The 'security umbrella' provided by the United States has, for eighty years, acted as a sedative for European ambition. It allowed us to maintain the illusion of sovereignty while relying on a foreign power for the most basic requirement of a state: the protection of its borders. As that umbrella frays—driven by a shifting US security posture and a growing internal American isolationism—the EU is panicking.

But the response is not to build a European Defence Union; it is to increase national spending. This is the 'Procurement Patchwork' in action. We are buying more of the same equipment, often from the same US suppliers, and calling it 'strategic autonomy.'

## Analysis: From Readiness to Union

Patria's view: The gap between our current state of 'readiness' and actual sovereignty is a chasm that cannot be bridged by more meetings or incremental budget increases. 

To close the Security-Sovereignty Gap, the EU must move beyond the intergovernmental trap. This requires three non-negotiable shifts:

1. **A Single European Defence Budget**: We must stop the inefficiency of 27 different procurement cycles. A federal budget for defence would allow for the strategic selection of capabilities, ending the wasteful duplication of national projects and the reliance on foreign off-the-shelf solutions.
2. **Unified Strategic Command**: We need a European command structure that is not a committee of generals from 27 nations, but a single authority capable of rapid deployment and strategic decision-making.
3. **A Sovereign Industrial Base**: Strategic autonomy is an empty phrase if we rely on the US for our intelligence, our satellites, and our most critical munitions. We must aggressively invest in a pan-European defence industry that prioritizes European needs over national export profits.

## Conclusion

'Defence readiness' is a dangerous illusion because it suggests we are closer to security than we actually are. As long as we remain a collection of national armies coordinated by a committee, we are not a sovereign power; we are a collection of vulnerabilities.

The window for the EU to move from reactive to proactive action is closing. If we continue to mistake coordination for integration, we will find that when the moment of truth arrives, we are not 'ready'—we are merely well-coordinated in our failure.
