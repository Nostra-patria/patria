# Patria — Visual Style Guide

Patria's header images reflect the same register as the writing: calm, diagnostic, structurally rigorous. The images do not celebrate or alarm — they illustrate the structural reality the article analyses.

---

## Core principle

**Show the problem, not the verdict.**

A Patria image shows the structural condition — the gap, the tension, the scale, the emptiness — not the outcome or the emotion. The viewer should feel like they are reading the situation, not receiving a message.

---

## What Patria images look like

**Style**: Architectural photography meets editorial illustration. Think *Economist* covers, *Der Spiegel* conceptual photography, or the flat analytical visual language of institutional reports — precise, slightly cold, composed.

**Colour palette**:
- Primary: Cold blues, institutional grey, matte steel
- Accent: Amber / gold (EU institutional yellow — used sparingly, never as a triumphant tone)
- Background: Deep navy, overcast sky, pre-dawn light
- Avoid: Warm sunsets, golden hour, fire, red political tones

**Lighting**:
- Overcast grey or cold blue dawn — not golden hour, not sunset
- Institutional: the flat diffuse light of a Brussels morning, not a campaign rally at dusk
- Dramatic is allowed only when it serves the structure, not the mood: a single point of light in a dark institutional space, not a blazing sky

**Subjects**:
- Infrastructure at scale: transmission towers, rail lines, pipelines, data centres, border crossings, port cranes, parliament buildings
- Empty or under-used institutions: empty chambers, unmanned border posts, half-empty conference tables
- Structural fragmentation: maps showing gaps, lines that stop at borders, puzzle pieces that don't meet
- Human scale vs. system scale: small figures dwarfed by institutional or industrial architecture — conveying governance complexity, not heroism

**Avoid**:
- Crowds celebrating, marching, or rallying
- Dramatic flag-waving (flags as background texture: OK — flags as protagonist: NO)
- Sunrise/sunset symbolism (progress narrative = propaganda register)
- Lightning, explosions, fire — unless the article is specifically about a literal crisis event
- Heroic single figures, leaders at podiums
- Map outlines with glowing EU borders (too nationalist-flag-aesthetic)
- Political poster aesthetics: bold diagonals, strong reds, clenched fists
- Digital/AI aesthetics for non-digital topics (glowing neural nets for energy articles = wrong)

## Prompt construction template

```
[Documentary/Editorial/Architectural] [photograph/illustration/aerial view]:
[Specific visual scene — infrastructure, space, or structural condition].
[What the scene conveys structurally — without narrating the political conclusion].
[European context marker if relevant — architecture, landscape, not flags].
[Lighting: cold blue dawn / overcast grey / institutional diffuse light].
[Scale marker if useful: small figures, long lines, vast infrastructure].
No text. No logos. No flags as protagonists. [Mood: analytical / diagnostic / austere].
```

---

## Anti-patterns (what to avoid in prompts)

| ❌ Do not write | ✅ Write instead |
|---|---|
| `glowing EU flags` | `high-voltage transmission lines crossing a European valley` |
| `triumphant crowd in European square` | `empty European Parliament chamber, morning light` |
| `dramatic sunrise over Brussels` | `overcast Brussels skyline, institutional grey light` |
| `AI neural network visualization` | `empty server room, cold blue light` |
| `lightning bolts and energy` | `transmission lines ending at a border, subtly misaligned` |
| `soldiers with European flags` | `unmanned military equipment clustered in national colour-coded sectors, aerial view` |
| `glowing golden Europe map` | `detailed relief map of Europe showing infrastructure gaps between countries` |
