# The Dependency Paradox: Why Europe's 'De-risking' is a Half-Measure

**In the corridors of the Berlaymont, 'de-risking' has become the mantra of the hour. It is a carefully crafted term, designed to signal a departure from the naive globalization of the past without triggering the geopolitical alarm bells of 'de-coupling.' By framing the reduction of dependencies on China and other adversarial powers as a matter of 'risk management' rather than 'strategic rupture,' the EU hopes to maintain the benefits of trade while insulating itself from coercion.**

**This is a dangerous illusion.**

## The Mirage of Risk Management

The fundamental flaw of the de-risking strategy is that it treats systemic dependency as a technical glitch that can be patched with a few new trade agreements. But dependency is not a glitch; it is the core of the current global economic architecture.

When the EU speaks of 'diversifying supply chains' for critical raw materials or semiconductors, it is often merely swapping one dependency for another. Trading a reliance on Chinese lithium for a reliance on Chilean or Australian mines does not inherently increase European autonomy—it simply changes the geography of the risk.

Strategic autonomy is not the absence of dependencies; it is the *control* over those dependencies. As long as the critical nodes of the global supply chain are owned or controlled by powers with fundamentally different political values and strategic goals, Europe remains a passenger in its own geopolitical vehicle.

## From Reactive to Proactive: The 2026 Window

We are currently in what can only be described as a 'narrow window of opportunity.' The fragmented world of 2026—defined by a shifting US security posture and an increasingly assertive China—demands a shift from a reactive to a proactive posture.

For too long, Europe has viewed its economic policy as separate from its security policy. We have allowed the 'market' to dictate our dependencies, and then tasked our diplomats with managing the fallout. This separation is an artifact of the post-Cold War era that no longer exists.

True strategic autonomy requires a 'Grand Strategy' that integrates economic policy with security objectives. This means:
1. **Industrial Sovereignty**: Moving beyond 'de-risking' to 're-shoring' and 'friend-shoring' critical production capacities within the EU, even if it means accepting higher short-term costs for long-term security.
2. **Technological Leadership**: Not just regulating the digital age, but leading it. We cannot be 'autonomous' if we rely on foreign-designed kernels and foreign-owned clouds for our most sensitive government functions.
3. **Geopolitical Courage**: The willingness to accept the economic pain of a structural break with adversarial powers in exchange for the ability to say 'no' without fear of systemic collapse.

## Analysis: The Federalist Link

Patria's view: The dependency paradox is a symptom of a fragmented Europe.

As long as the EU's trade and industrial policies are fragmented across 27 different national interests, we will always be the easiest target for coercion. China and the US do not negotiate with 'the European Union' as a monolithic entity; they negotiate with individual member states, exploiting the cracks in our unity to ensure our continued dependency.

The only way to solve the dependency paradox is to federalize the EU's strategic assets. A single, unified European Industrial Policy—backed by a common treasury and a single geopolitical voice—would transform Europe from a collection of dependent markets into a sovereign global power.

## Conclusion

De-risking is a half-measure for a half-hearted project. It is an attempt to keep the comforts of the old world while ignoring the realities of the new one. If Europe continues to treat its dependencies as a risk-management exercise, it will find that the risks have not been managed, but merely accumulated.

The time for linguistic compromises is over. It is time for Europe to choose: either it remains a high-standard regulatory zone for the world's great powers, or it becomes a sovereign power in its own right.
